# CHAPTER 7: The Pro Per at War — Rosario Prepares for Trial Alone

---

## Narrative

When the Dolan Law Firm withdrew, Rosario did what almost no one in his position does: he continued the litigation himself, in pro per. One brain-injured man against one of the largest automobile insurers in California and two retained defense firms. The asymmetry was structural: unlimited legal resources on one side, a man whose traumatic brain injury had degraded his executive function on the other. Every filing, every deadline, every exhibit required planning, sequencing, and sustained focus. The continuance denial had compressed his preparation time. He could not depose the officers. He could not authenticate the clips. He could not organize a coherent presentation. The [Second Amended Complaint](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/02-CASE-CGC-25-631801/01-Second-Amended-Complaint.pdf) named seven defendants. Service on each. CRC formatting. Exhibit sets. He knew the system was rigged against him. He went to trial anyway. The alternative was to let the lies stand. He brought to the legal fight the same analytical rigor that had defined his career as a senior AI systems architect. He would need every bit of it.

---

### I. The Decision to Continue

The Dolan Law Firm's withdrawal happened in August 2024. The case had been in litigation for over three years. Trial was scheduled for early 2025. Rosario was left with a transfer of materials, an evidentiary record developed through years of discovery, and the choice of whether to continue the litigation in pro per or to allow it to collapse. The practical obstacles to continuing were formidable. The TBI had not resolved. The cognitive overhead of litigation management had not lessened. The institutional resources arrayed against him had not diminished. And the preparation window was shrinking.

The choice to continue was not naive optimism. It was a deliberate calculation based on what the record contained. Rosario had the deposition transcripts. He had the discovery production. He had the Dolan materials. He had, in the certified transcript of the proceeding to that point, a factual record that he believed would ultimately establish what happened. The system had failed him in the sense that his counsel had withdrawn at a critical moment, but the record the proceeding had built was intact. He could read it. He could analyze it. He could see what was there. And what was there, he calculated, was enough.

This is the engineer's approach to litigation. An engineer who encounters a failed system does not assume the failure is irreparable. The engineer reads the logs, traces the error, identifies the root cause, and builds a solution. Rosario applied the same methodology to the trial record. He read the transcripts. He traced the evidentiary chain. He identified the critical moments where the false representations had corrupted the proceeding. And he built, from the defense's own record, the argument that would eventually form the foundation of three independent proceedings. He went to trial in pro per not because he had no choice, but because he saw what the record contained and understood that the record, properly deployed, was sufficient.

### II. The Procedural Reality of Self-Representation

Civil litigation in California is governed by CRC formatting, CCP procedure, and local rules. CRC rule 3.1110(f) requires exhibit tab dividers, sequential numbering, and sets for court, jury, clerk, and each party. In Rosario's case, that meant sets for seven defendants plus court and clerk. CCP section 1010 governs service; each defendant required proper service. *Faretta v. California* (1975) 422 U.S. 806 recognizes the right to self-representation in criminal cases; civil litigants may proceed in pro per but are held to the same procedural standards as counsel.

The deposition transcripts had to be excerpted for impeachment purposes. The body-worn camera footage had to be organized into the clips most relevant to the key issues. The 911 recording had to be prepared for presentation in a form the jury could hear clearly. The CAD logs had to be organized so their evidentiary significance was apparent. All of this required not just time, but a specific kind of organized, sequential cognitive effort that the TBI had made significantly more costly. The preparation that an attorney with a support staff could accomplish in a matter of days required from Rosario a sustained effort over weeks, under conditions of cognitive overhead that would have been disabling to most people.

The continuance denial compressed the available preparation time. The continuance Rosario had requested was not a delay tactic. It was a request for the time that the false representations had stolen from him. He had not been able to depose the officers whose body-worn cameras captured the scene, because the pretrial dispute about the provenance of the evidence had consumed the preparation window. He had not been able to authenticate the 911 recording through custodial testimony, because the continuance he needed for that process had been denied. He entered the courtroom with what he had: the materials, the transcripts, the Compex subpoena, and the analytical framework he had built from reading the record.

### III. Seven Defendants and the Weight of Service

The equity complaint in CGC-25-631801, the [Second Amended Complaint](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/02-CASE-CGC-25-631801/01-Second-Amended-Complaint.pdf), named seven defendants: Subhi Abdelhalim, CSAA Insurance Exchange, Phillips Spallas & Angstadt LLP, Priya Navaratnasingham, Alberto Reyna, Michael R. Chambers, and Carbone Smith & Koyama. Each defendant required proper service. Service of process in California requires personal delivery to an authorized agent or, under certain circumstances, substituted service by leaving process at the address and mailing a copy. For a law firm, service is typically made on a member of the firm or the firm's registered agent for service of process. For an individual defendant, service requires locating the individual and effecting personal delivery.

The logistics of serving seven defendants, some of them individual attorneys at law firms with multiple office addresses, some of them corporate entities with designated agents, required coordination with process servers, verification of addresses, tracking of service returns, and management of a proof of service file for each defendant. The filing fees for the equity action and the fraud action were substantial. Reporter's transcripts cost thousands of dollars. Certified copies of court records require fees. The copying and printing costs for court filings ran into hundreds of dollars per filing. The out-of-pocket costs of the litigation, documented in the demand letter and associated support documents, were significant and compounding.

Rosario managed all of this himself. Without a support staff, without billing software, without a paralegal to track deadlines. With a brain injury that made every cognitive task more expensive than it would have been before February 4, 2021. The sheer administrative burden of managing a multi-defendant, multi-action litigation in pro per, while simultaneously preparing for the trial of the underlying case, is a dimension of this story that the legal documents cannot fully capture. What the documents capture is the output: the filings, the exhibits, the briefs, the strategic architecture that turned the defense's own record into the proof of their fraud. The quality of that output reflects what the man who produced it was capable of, even under the conditions that the collision and the fraud had imposed.

---

### IV. The Engineer at the Keyboard

Rosario brought to the litigation the tools and methods of a senior AI systems architect. He used technology systematically to manage the record. He built document databases. He created timelines. He mapped the evidentiary chain in structured formats that allowed him to trace each false representation to its specific transcript citation and to identify the court ruling that each representation had produced. The Reliance Matrix, which appears in the case repository's support documents and which is discussed in detail in Chapter 19, is a product of this systematic approach. Seven links. Each row: false representation, date, transcript citation, court ruling, harm. This is not the work of a disorganized pro per litigant. This is the work of a systems analyst who applied his professional methodology to a legal problem.

The analytical rigor that produced the Reliance Matrix also produced the other documents in the case repository: the Fatal Proof document cataloging every contradicting statement and its transcript citation; the Narrative Effect of Misrepresentations on Plaintiff tracing the causal chain from each false representation to each downstream harm; the Journal Article situating the case in the broader context of advanced civil litigation strategy. These documents were not produced by an attorney with institutional support. They were produced by one man, with a TBI, working from the certified transcript of a proceeding in which the adversary system had been corrupted against him.

The legal architecture that eventually emerged, three independent proceedings each with its own theory, decision-maker, and remedy, is described in Chapters 14 through 19 of this book. Its strategic sophistication has been noted in the Journal Article published in the case repository. But its origin is simpler than its sophistication might suggest. It began with one man reading the transcript of his own trial. Seeing what was there. Understanding what it meant. And refusing to accept it.

The alternative was to let the lies stand. He would not. He went to trial in pro per against a multi-billion-dollar insurer and two law firms, with a traumatic brain injury, without counsel, without a support staff, without the preparation time the false representations had stolen. He did it because the record contained the proof, and the proof deserved to be heard.
