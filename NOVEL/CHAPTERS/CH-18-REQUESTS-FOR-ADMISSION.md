# CHAPTER 18: Requests for Admission — The Binary Compulsion Machine

---

## Narrative

Requests for admission served simultaneously with the complaint. Binary compulsion: each request constructed so admission or specific denial creates a problem. Admit the denial letter stated investigation "concluded" -- representation established. Deny -- the document contradicts the denial. Admit had not obtained 911 before denial -- falsity established. Deny -- discovery into the claim file tests it. The insurer must maintain a "concluded" investigation while its agents claimed "no idea" where the 911 came from. These positions cannot both be true. The [Requests for Admission](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/02-CASE-CGC-25-631801/06-Requests-for-Admission.pdf) and [631802 RFAs](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/03-CASE-CGC-25-631802/06-Requests-for-Admission.pdf) create early pressure. The thirty-day statutory deadline runs from service. The defendant must choose positions before full landscape review. Early positions become commitments. The pressure is immediate.

Requests for admission are among the most underused tools in civil discovery. Most practitioners deploy them defensively, asking for admissions of uncontested facts to narrow the issues at trial. Rosario used them offensively, as a pressure system: a set of binary questions, each calibrated so that the defendant's only options are to admit a damaging fact or deny a provable one. The structure is mechanical. The pressure is legal, not rhetorical. Each admission creates a binding judicial admission. Each denial creates a commitment the defendant must maintain under oath, and if the denial is false, the documents prove it.

---

### I. The Design Philosophy: Binary Compulsion

Binary compulsion is the design principle. For each key factual proposition, the RFA is drafted so that both the admission and the denial advance the plaintiff's theory. An admission establishes the proposition as a judicial admission, binding on the party for all purposes in the litigation. A denial that contradicts a document creates impeachment material: when the deponent later testifies, the false denial can be shown to be false by the document it contradicts, and the false denial itself becomes evidence of consciousness of guilt, knowledge of the adverse fact, and willingness to misrepresent. In either case, the plaintiff has gained something.

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.250 requires responses within thirty days of service. The defendant must choose positions before full investigation, before depositions, before the full picture of plaintiff's proof. Under *Burch v. Gombos* (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 356, 364, admissions are binding judicial admissions. Early positions become commitments that constrain deposition and trial options.

The simultaneous service of RFAs with the complaint maximizes this pressure. Most defendants expect to have the time between service and the start of discovery to assess the case before being required to commit to factual positions. When RFAs arrive with the complaint, the defendant is committed to positions on the core facts before the litigation has fully developed. If those positions are later shown to be false, the false RFA responses become evidence in the fraud action, the equity action, and the bar complaints simultaneously.

---

### II. The Denial Letter Requests

The denial letter RFAs target the core factual predicate of the fraud action. The denial letter stated that CSAA had completed a "careful and thorough investigation" and had "concluded" that the claim was denied. The RFAs ask CSAA to admit or deny specific propositions that follow logically from the letter's representations:

Admit that the denial letter stated that CSAA had "concluded" an investigation into the incident. This is quotation from the letter itself. CSAA must admit it. The letter says what it says. An admission establishes the representation as a judicial admission.

Admit that the denial letter was sent twenty-one days after the February 4, 2021 incident. This is a date-specific factual proposition that can be verified from the letter's date and the incident's date. CSAA must admit it. An admission establishes the timeline.

Admit that prior to issuing the denial letter, CSAA had not obtained the 911 recording from SFPD. This is the key proposition. If CSAA admits it, the falsity of the "thorough investigation" claim is established: an investigation that does not include the recording of the incident's immediate aftermath is not thorough. If CSAA denies it, the denial invites discovery of the claim file to determine whether a 911 recording request appears in the file before the denial letter date. If no such request appears, the denial was false. If a request appears, CSAA must explain why the recording was not mentioned in the denial letter or referenced in the investigation file. Either path advances the plaintiff's theory.

Admit that prior to issuing the denial letter, CSAA had not obtained the body-worn camera footage from SFPD. Same structure. Same binary. Admit: incompleteness of investigation established. Deny: discovery of claim file determines whether BWC footage was sought, and if not, the denial was false.

---

### III. The Evidence Possession Requests

The equity action RFAs target the core factual predicate of the extrinsic fraud theory. They ask the law firm defendants to admit or deny propositions about their possession and knowledge of the Dolan production:

Admit that in May 2023, Phillips Spallas & Angstadt received the litigation file from Carbone Smith & Koyama. This is a background fact about the substitution. Either admission or denial is manageable, but admission is more likely: the substitution is documented.

Admit that the litigation file received from Carbone Smith & Koyama included materials produced by the Dolan Law Firm in discovery. This is the key possession question. If PSA admits it, the possession is established. If PSA denies it, the denial is contradicted by the "provided by Dolan" admission Navaratnasingham made at Trial Day 7, and the Jessup testimony that the materials were produced to prior defense counsel.

Admit that prior to February 18, 2025, attorneys at Phillips Spallas & Angstadt had reviewed the body-worn camera footage produced by the Dolan Law Firm. The "provided by Dolan" admission, combined with the evidence that PSA had edited the footage to eight or nine minutes for trial presentation, establishes that PSA had reviewed the footage before the February 18 hearing. An admission confirms the knowing nature of the "news to me" representation. A denial is contradicted by the evidence of the trial presentation, which required pre-trial review and editing.

---

### IV. The Internally Inconsistent Position

The deepest pressure created by the RFAs is the requirement to maintain positions that are internally inconsistent across different defendants or across different proceedings. CSAA's "concluded" investigation claim requires the insurer to maintain that its investigation was thorough and complete. PSA's "news to me" claim requires the law firm to maintain that it had not received the 911 recording until the day of the MSC. These two positions are in logical tension: if the investigation was thorough and complete, and the 911 recording was the most probative piece of evidence about what happened at the scene, why did the thorough investigation not include the recording? And if the recording existed and was obtainable, and the defense attorneys somehow did not have it until the day of the MSC, what does that say about the thoroughness of the investigation that produced the denial letter?

The RFAs force each defendant to commit to answers that, taken together, create an internally inconsistent picture. The insurer claims thorough investigation. The defense attorneys claim ignorance of the most probative evidence. Both cannot be true. The RFA responses, when examined together, reveal the contradiction that the three-front litigation is designed to exploit.
