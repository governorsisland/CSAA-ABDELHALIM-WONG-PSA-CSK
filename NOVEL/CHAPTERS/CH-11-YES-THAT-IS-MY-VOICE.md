# CHAPTER 11: "Yes. That Is My Voice."

---

## Narrative

---

### I. Trial Day 8: The Witness Chair

On the morning of April 17, 2025, Subhi Abdelhalim walked to the witness stand in Department 504 of the San Francisco Superior Court, placed his hand on a Bible, and swore to tell the truth.

He had made this walk before. He had been deposed years earlier, during the period when the Dolan Law Firm still represented Rosario and when the body-worn camera footage and 911 recording had been available and used. He had answered questions under oath then, and he would answer them again now. But between the deposition and this moment, everything had changed. The Dolan firm had withdrawn. Rosario had been left to fight alone. The pretrial motions had redrawn the battlefield. The evidence that should have framed Abdelhalim's testimony, the 911 confession heard first, the body cameras providing visual corroboration, the supplemental report filling the documentary gaps, had been excluded, deferred, or presented on the defense's terms. The jury that watched Abdelhalim take the stand had been carefully shielded from the unified evidentiary picture that would have made his testimony impossible to sustain.

Rosario sat at the plaintiff's table. Alone. No co-counsel to pass notes. No paralegal to cue exhibits. No second chair to catch the contradictions in real time. A brain-injured man in a suit, with a laptop and a stack of transcript excerpts, about to cross-examine the man who had struck him in a crosswalk four years earlier and left him to die on cold pavement.

The fluorescent lights of the courtroom hummed at the same frequency as the hospital room where Rosario had spent the first weeks after the collision. He noticed. He always noticed now. The TBI had stripped his brain of its sensory filters, and the courtroom, with its mechanical ventilation, its rustling papers, its creaking chairs, its low murmur of spectators shifting in hard wooden pews, was a gauntlet of stimuli that a healthy brain would have filtered to silence. Rosario's brain could not. Every sound competed for his attention. Every sound cost him cognitive resources he could not spare.

Abdelhalim settled into the witness chair. His attorneys had prepared him. They had reviewed the themes, rehearsed the framing, coached the language. He was insured by CSAA. The defense was funded by CSAA. The attorneys who had spent months telling the court that the 911 recording was "unknown" and of "uncertain provenance" were CSAA's retained counsel. Abdelhalim was, in every functional sense, a mouthpiece for an institutional defense strategy that had been architected to keep the jury from hearing his own words.

---

### II. "No Contact"

The defense theory was simple, and it had been simple from the beginning. No meaningful contact. The pedestrian fell. The van may have been in the vicinity, but the forces involved were insufficient to produce the injuries Rosario claimed. The defense's biomechanical expert had testified to this over the preceding days, a credentialed professional offering opinions about impact physics and injury thresholds, opinions that filled the evidentiary void created by the excluded supplemental report and the deferred 911 recording.

Abdelhalim testified consistently with this theory. He had testified before, at deposition, about the events of February 4, 2021. The defense team had structured his trial testimony to reinforce the "no contact" or "minimal contact" narrative. The intersection. The turn. The moment. His account minimized the event to the point of erasure. In the telling his lawyers had prepared, the collision was barely a collision at all.

The jury had been listening for eight days. They had heard the defense witnesses. They had heard the defense expert. They had seen the body-worn camera footage, eight or nine minutes of it, curated by the defense from five full-length videos, presented by Navaratnasingham on the defense's terms, edited to show what the defense wanted the jury to see. The footage had been "provided by Dolan," as Navaratnasingham had admitted the day before, but it was presented as if the defense had generously made it available, not as if they had been sitting on it for years while claiming ignorance.

The jury had not heard the 911 recording in context. They had not heard the supplemental report. They had not been given the Vehicle Code sections they had asked about. They had heard the defense's version of reality, carefully constructed over eight trial days, before they heard the defendant's own words contradicting it.

This is how suppression works. Not through the dramatic act of a document being burned or a witness being silenced. Through timing. Through sequence. Through the careful management of when evidence arrives in the jury's consciousness. A confession heard first, before the denial, frames the denial as what it is: a lie. A confession heard last, after eight days of denial, is heard through the lens of the denial. The defense understood this. That is why they fought so hard to keep the recording out of the plaintiff's hands and out of the plaintiff's presentation sequence.

---

### III. The Recording Is Played

The moment came in the afternoon of Trial Day 8. The 911 recording, the same recording that had been the subject of MIL No. 17, the same recording that Navaratnasingham had called "news to me," the same recording that Reyna had professed not to recognize, the same recording that had been deferred by the court pending Rosario's testimony, was finally played in the courtroom.

The audio filled the room. The voice of a man on a phone. The dispatcher's questions. And then the words.

"I barely hit him."

The courtroom did not gasp. Real courtrooms do not gasp like courtrooms in movies. The jurors sat. The judge sat. The defense attorneys sat. Rosario sat. The words hung in the air of Department 504 like a bell struck in a quiet room, reverberating not because anyone reacted but because the words themselves carried a weight that required no reaction. They were what they were. The defendant's own voice. The defendant's own admission. Made at the scene. Made before lawyers. Made before strategy. Made before the twenty-one-day denial letter. Made before the motions in limine. Made before "news to me." Made before everything that had been constructed to keep this moment from happening.

"I barely hit him."

Four words. The word "barely" doing its desperate work, not denying contact but trying to minimize it, like a man confessing to a crime while simultaneously negotiating the sentence. "Barely" is the confession's fingerprint. It acknowledges the act. It is the word of a man who knows what he did and is already trying to make it smaller. "I barely hit him." I did it, but not really. I struck him, but not hard. I confess, but please do not hold me to the full weight of the confession.

The recording played. The courtroom heard it. And then came the question.

---

### IV. Five Words

Rosario, representing himself, standing at the podium with his transcript excerpts and his damaged brain and four years of litigation behind him, asked the question.

Is that your voice?

Abdelhalim sat in the witness chair. He was under oath. He had sworn to tell the truth. The recording had just played. The voice on the recording was his. He knew it was his. His attorneys knew it was his. The court knew it was his. Everyone in the room knew it was his. The question was not a question of genuine uncertainty. It was a formality required by the rules of authentication. Under Evidence Code section 1220, a party admission requires only that the statement be made by a party and offered against that party. Authentication, confirming that the voice on the recording belongs to the person against whom it is offered, is the foundational step.

Abdelhalim could have equivocated. He could have said he was not sure. He could have said the recording was unclear. He could have said he could not remember. His attorneys had spent months constructing a narrative in which this recording was of "unknown provenance," in which the defense had "no idea" where it came from, in which counsel was "not sure what recording" the plaintiff was referring to. The entire pretrial strategy had been built to avoid this exact moment. Every motion, every hearing representation, every trial-day objection had been aimed at preventing the jury from hearing this recording in context, and if the recording was heard, at preventing the defendant from being forced to authenticate it on the stand.

All of that effort. All of those false representations. All of those court rulings procured through fraud. And now the recording had played, and the question had been asked, and Subhi Abdelhalim was under oath, and there was nowhere left to hide.

His answer, preserved at Reporter's Transcript, Trial Day 8, line 1979:

"Yes. That is my voice."

Five words. The defendant authenticated his own party admission. The man whose attorneys had told the court the recording was "unknown" confirmed under oath that the voice on the recording was his. The voice that said "I barely hit him." His voice. His words. His confession.

The same attorneys who had signed MIL No. 17 under penalty of perjury, claiming the defendant had "no idea where this 911 call log or audio recording came from," now sat at the defense table while their client told the court it was his voice. The same attorney who had said "news to me" at the February 18 hearing sat at the defense table while the defendant confirmed the recording she had claimed not to have received. The same attorney who had issued a Compex subpoena fourteen months earlier requesting this exact recording by name sat at the defense table while the defendant confirmed that the recording was exactly what it had always been: his voice, his words, his confession.

The four words from the 911 call were now authenticated evidence. The party admission was formally in the record. The foundational fact of the case, that the defendant had struck the plaintiff in the crosswalk, was established through the defendant's own mouth, in two separate forms: the excited utterance on the 911 call, and the under-oath authentication at trial.

---

### V. The Damage Already Done

But by then, the damage was done.

The jury had heard Abdelhalim's "no contact" testimony before they heard the recording. Eight days of defense-constructed narrative had been poured into the jury's consciousness before the four words arrived to contradict it. In cognitive science, this is called the anchoring effect: the first piece of information a person receives about a subject exerts disproportionate influence on subsequent judgments, even when later information contradicts it. The defense understood this. That is why they fought to defer the recording. That is why they fought to present their evidence first. That is why every pretrial maneuver was aimed at ensuring that the jury's first impression of the collision came from the defense's witnesses, the defense's expert, the defense's curated body-camera footage, and the defendant's own carefully prepared "no contact" testimony, not from the defendant's own words on the 911 call.

The supplemental report, which would have corroborated Rosario's account of the collision with additional law enforcement narrative, was excluded. The body-worn camera footage, five full-length videos showing the scene, the officers, the witnesses, the aftermath, had been presented by the defense in an eight-or-nine-minute curated edit. The governing Vehicle Code sections that would have given the jury a legal framework for evaluating the defendant's conduct had been withheld when the jury asked for them. The 911 recording arrived at the end, alone, without the supporting structure that would have given it its full force.

A capstone without a building beneath it. That is what the authentication moment was. The most powerful piece of evidence in the case, the defendant's own words, authenticated by the defendant himself, arrived in the jury's consciousness after the narrative framework had already been set by eight days of defense evidence. It arrived without the supplemental report. It arrived without the full body-camera footage. It arrived without the statutory duty framework. It arrived without the preparation time that would have allowed Rosario to build a coherent, sequential, devastating evidentiary presentation anchored by the 911 call.

Rosario had tried to play the recording earlier. Reyna had objected: "I am not sure what recording he is referring to." The court had deferred it. The deferral ensured that the recording arrived late. The lateness ensured that the defense narrative was already anchored. The anchoring ensured that the authentication, when it finally came, had less force than it would have had if the jury had heard the defendant's own words at the beginning of the liability case, before the defense had eight days to construct a narrative that the recording demolished.

This is what the fraud cost. Not the suppression of evidence in the absolute sense, the recording was eventually played, the defendant eventually authenticated it. The fraud's cost was temporal. It was sequential. It was structural. The evidence arrived, but it arrived in the wrong place in the story the jury was being told. And in litigation, as in narrative, sequence is meaning. The same facts, presented in a different order, tell a different story. The defense ensured that its story was told first.

---

### VI. What the Authentication Proved

The authentication proved more than the jury knew.

The jury heard "Yes. That is my voice" as a factual confirmation. The defendant's voice. The defendant's words. Relevant to the question of whether contact occurred. That is what the jury could evaluate, and three of the twelve jurors apparently found it persuasive despite its late arrival.

But the authentication also proved something the jury never saw: that every pretrial representation about the recording's provenance had been false. The defense had told the court the recording was "unknown." The defendant just confirmed it was his voice. The defense had told the court it had "no idea where it came from." The defendant just confirmed it was a 911 call he made from the scene. The defense had told the court the recording had "not been produced in the normal course of discovery." Navaratnasingham had admitted, the day before, that the body cameras from the same production were "provided by Dolan." The recording was not unknown. It was not unproduced. It was not mysterious. It was the defendant's own voice on a 911 call he made from the scene of the collision, captured by the SFPD dispatch system, obtained by the Dolan firm through routine public records requests, produced to the defense in discovery, used in the defendant's deposition, and sitting in the defense file for nearly two years while the defense told the court it did not exist.

"Yes. That is my voice." The defendant said it. His lawyers heard it. The court heard it. The transcript recorded it at line 1979 of Trial Day 8. The four words from the Prologue, "I barely hit him", were now doubly authenticated: once by the defendant at the scene, spontaneously, in the stress of the moment; and once by the defendant on the stand, under oath, four years later. The first authentication was evidentiary. The second was forensic. Together, they established, beyond any reasonable dispute, that the recording was what Rosario had always said it was, that the defendant had said what Rosario had always claimed he said, and that every representation to the contrary had been false.

The jury returned its verdict six days later. Nine to three. Defense prevails. The minimum civil majority. One juror's mind, changed by any piece of the suppressed or fragmented evidence, the recording heard first instead of last, the supplemental report admitted instead of excluded, the Vehicle Code provisions applied instead of withheld, would have altered the outcome.

The defendant authenticated his own admission. The truth reached the courtroom. It reached the jury. But it arrived too late, too fragmented, too stripped of context, in a trial whose evidentiary architecture had been corrupted before the first witness was sworn.

"Yes. That is my voice."

The four words and the five words. The confession and its confirmation. Separated by four years and by a wall of institutional fraud. The transcript holds them both.
