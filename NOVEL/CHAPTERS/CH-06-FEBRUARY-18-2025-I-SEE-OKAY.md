# CHAPTER 6: February 18, 2025 — "I See. Okay."

---

## Narrative

On February 18, 2025, the question of evidence possession came to a head. Jeremy Jessup of the Dolan Law Firm took the stand at the pretrial hearing before Judge Garrett L. Wong in Department 504 of the San Francisco Superior Court. Under oath, he testified that "body cam footage, about five videos, as well as the 911 audio, as well as the CAD log" had been "produced to defense counsel" and "served on them in the production for discovery." The materials were "utilized in the deposition of defendants." He was an officer of the court. His testimony was unambiguous. Navaratnasingham responded. Not with a request to investigate, but with a denial: "So this is news to me. We didn't realize that prior to handling counsel may have received it... I can represent to you that our office did not receive the 911 call until the day of the MSC." The court said: "I see. Okay." Those three words were the sound of a court accepting a representation from an officer of the court at face value. Judge Wong did not order an inquiry. He did not demand a declaration. He accepted what she told him. After a recess, she reinforced: "I am representing to this Court that we did not have those two items until the day of the MSC." The motion to continue was denied. Rosario would enter trial without the time he needed to depose officers, authenticate clips, or prepare a coherent presentation. The [hearing transcript](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/HEARINGS/All-Hearing-Transcripts-Rosario-AAA.pdf) preserves every word. The court said "I see. Okay" because it trusted its officers. Those officers betrayed the trust. The plaintiff paid the price.

---

### I. The Stage: Department 504

The February 18, 2025 pretrial hearing was not a minor scheduling conference. It was the occasion at which the most consequential factual dispute in the case, whether the 911 recording and related materials had been produced to defense counsel, would be resolved by the trial judge who would also preside over the trial itself. The judge's ruling on this question would determine whether Rosario would have the time to prepare an orderly evidentiary presentation, whether the materials would be treated as properly authenticated and admissible, and whether the continuance Rosario needed to complete that preparation would be granted.

The hearing had three participants with distinct roles. Jeremy Jessup was former counsel for Rosario, there as a subpoenaed witness to testify about what the Dolan firm had done. Navaratnasingham was current defense counsel, there to represent her client's interests. Rosario was the plaintiff, representing himself, with a traumatic brain injury, managing his own case against an institutional defense team. Judge Wong was presiding, applying the law to the facts as he understood them based on the representations made in court.

The fundamental asymmetry of the situation is important to understand. Jessup had direct, firsthand knowledge of what had been produced. Navaratnasingham had access to her firm's records of what it had received. Rosario had his own knowledge of what he had submitted and when. The court had the ability to require documentary evidence rather than accepting oral representations alone. None of the safeguards that could have prevented the fraud from taking root were deployed. The court accepted Navaratnasingham's verbal denial of Jessup's sworn testimony without requiring any corroboration.

---

### II. Jessup's Testimony: Fixed and Unimpeached

At hearing transcript pages 139-140, Jessup testified under oath: "body cam footage, about five videos, as well as the 911 audio, as well as the CAD log" were "produced to defense counsel" and "served on them in the production for discovery" and "utilized in the deposition of defendants." He acknowledged he did not know whether CSK transmitted the materials to PSA at substitution. His testimony has never been impeached.

---

### III. "News to Me" and the Architecture of Denial

Navaratnasingham's response was a carefully constructed denial. The full text from the hearing transcript at page 141, lines 4-11: "So this is news to me. We didn't realize that prior to handling counsel may have received it, and I can't confirm whether that was produced. I take counsel's word, of course, but we, I can represent to you that our office did not receive the 911 call until the day of the MSC, the day before the MSC and that was an attachment link."

Notice the structure. She does not dispute that prior counsel may have received the materials. She draws a distinction between what prior counsel may have had and what "our office" received. This distinction is the legal theory behind the denial: PSA was not CSK; PSA received the file at substitution but claims it did not receive the specific materials until the MSC. The substitution, in her framing, is a clean break that separates PSA from the consequences of CSK's possession.

The problem with this framing, and it is a fatal problem, is that file transfers do not work that way. The file transfers with the contents. Constructive receipt of the file is constructive receipt of its contents. A law firm that receives a client file containing twenty deposition transcripts and a Redweld labeled "Dolan Discovery Production" is in possession of those transcripts and that production. Professional responsibility rules require incoming counsel to review what they receive. The claim that PSA received the file but somehow did not receive the materials within it is not a legal defense. It is, at best, a description of professional negligence. At worst, it is a knowing false statement to the court.

After a recess, Navaratnasingham reinforced her position at pages 163-165: "I am representing to this Court that we did not have those two items until the day of the MSC and those items were produced in connection with the settlement to the settlement officer." And at pages 176-177, she attempted to reframe Jessup's testimony: "According to Mr. Jessup, the body cam footage, the 911 call, all this stuff has been in his possession since August. The motion in limine that I filed was saying that the 911 call was not produced in the normal course of discovery." This is the rhetorical inversion: Jessup testified that he produced the materials to defense counsel. She reframed that as Rosario having sat on materials since August. The reframing is false. Jessup's testimony was about what he did. It was not about Rosario's possession timeline.

---

### IV. The Three Words and Their Consequence

The court's response, preserved at page 141, line 15: "I see. Okay." Three words. The sound of a system that trusts its officers. Business and Professions Code section 6068(d) requires that an attorney "never seek to mislead the judge or any judicial officer by an artifice or false statement of fact or law." California Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 3.3(a)(1) prohibits false statements to a tribunal and requires correction of any material false statement. As *In re Silverton* (2005) 36 Cal.4th 81, 91 recognizes, the duty of candor is fundamental to the administration of justice. The system functions because that duty is observed. When it is violated, the court becomes an unwitting instrument of the violation.

The court denied the continuance. At page 181, the transcript records the ruling. The court's stated reasoning, at pages 178 and 182, was that Rosario had had the materials "since August of 2024" and that the defense was "not blocking" Rosario from seeking admission. Every element of that reasoning was false. The defense had had the materials since 2022. They had filed motions premised on false claims of non-receipt. They had told the court the materials were "news to me." The defense did not need to overtly block Rosario. The false representations were the blocking mechanism, operating through the court's rulings. The continuance denial deprived Rosario of the preparation time he needed to present the evidence in a coherent, contextualized form. It deprived him of the officer depositions that would have authenticated the body-worn camera footage through custodial testimony. It deprived him of the time to prepare exhibit sets, video clips, and audio transcriptions that would have allowed the jury to engage with the evidence effectively. That harm is the downstream consequence of "I see. Okay."
