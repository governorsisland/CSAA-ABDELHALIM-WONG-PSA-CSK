# CHAPTER 8: Trial Day 7, Part One — The Supplemental Report

---

## Narrative

On April 16, 2025, Trial Day 7, the courtroom had reached the part of the proceeding that most determined the shape of what the jury would ultimately hear and believe. Rosario, representing himself, was attempting to present the evidentiary case for liability. His injuries. The collision. The physics of impact at a crosswalk. The defendant's speed and direction. The governing Vehicle Code provisions. And, embedded within that evidentiary structure, the documents that the Dolan Law Firm had obtained from the San Francisco Police Department in 2022: the 911 recording, the body-worn camera footage, the CAD logs, and the supplemental police report.

The supplemental report was the first document Rosario attempted to introduce. It contained additional narrative about the collision that would have corroborated his account and challenged the defense's theory that no meaningful contact had occurred. He marked it as an exhibit. He offered it to the court. Navaratnasingham objected.

Her words are at Reporter's Transcript, Trial Day 7, around line 2874: "Your Honor, for the record, I do want to just state, as far as this document goes, this is not -- we subpoenaed the police records for the incident and this is not anything that we received as part of the subpoena."

The court sustained the objection. "It is not evidence. It is not in the report." RT 7, lines 2888-2896. The supplemental report was excluded. Rosario would not be able to use it to corroborate his account of the impact. The defense theory of "no contact" would be challenged only by the other materials he had available, materials that were themselves being presented out of sequence and without the preparation time he had needed.

Minutes later, the court asked about the body-worn camera footage on the defense's own exhibit list. Navaratnasingham responded: "Sure. They were provided by Dolan. He has had them for whatever, sure."

The two statements, separated by minutes of trial time, contain everything. She had claimed the supplemental report was not received via the defense's subpoena. That was technically framed to create the impression that the defense did not have the materials. Then she admitted the body-worn camera footage had been "provided by Dolan," the same Dolan whose production Jessup had testified included the 911 recording, the body-worn camera footage, and the CAD logs as a unified package. The artifice had collapsed before it could be properly assembled. ### I. April 16, 2025: The Trial in Progress

By Trial Day 7, the litigation was in its fourth year. Rosario had been navigating the proceeding without counsel for eight months. He had managed the pretrial motions, the exhibit preparation, the opening statement, and the examination of the witnesses who had testified in the first six days. The TBI that had been his constant companion since February 4, 2021 had not resolved. The executive function deficits that affected his working memory, attention shifting, and cognitive sequencing were still present. Every day of trial required more of him than it would have required of an attorney without an injury.

The continuance denial had consequences that played out across the trial days. He had not been able to depose the SFPD officers whose body-worn cameras captured the scene. The officers' testimony would have established the custodial foundation for the footage and contextualized the scene in ways that would have been powerful for the jury. Without that deposition preparation, Rosario was presenting footage that lacked the custodial authentication testimony that would have placed it in its most compelling context. He had not been able to prepare the kind of organized, sequenced evidentiary presentation that defense counsel, with their institutional support and unlimited preparation time, could produce.

Against this backdrop, the supplemental police report was an important piece of evidence. It was a document generated by the SFPD. Under Evidence Code section 1280, a writing made as a record of an act, condition, or event is not inadmissible as hearsay if it was made by and within the scope of duty of a public employee, at or near the time of the act, and the sources of information and method of preparation indicate trustworthiness. Police supplemental reports qualify. Their content would have provided additional narrative corroboration for Rosario's account.

Rosario had obtained this document as part of the police records collection that the Dolan Law Firm had compiled from the SFPD. It was part of the same production that included the 911 recording, the body-worn camera footage, and the CAD logs. He had it. He had had it since Dolan produced it to him as part of the transfer of his litigation file. He offered it.

---

### II. The "Subpoena Shield" Objection: Anatomy of an Artifice

Navaratnasingham's objection was precisely worded. She did not say the document was inadmissible. She did not argue it lacked foundation. She did not contend it was hearsay without a qualifying exception. She said: "We subpoenaed the police records for the incident and this is not anything that we received as part of the subpoena."

This is the subpoena shield. The structure of the objection is designed to create an impression without making a direct assertion. The impression: we went to the authoritative source (SFPD), we got what there was to get, and this document was not in what we received; therefore, this document's provenance is uncertain. The direct assertion: we received this specific document through a specific channel (subpoena) and it was not there.

The problem with the subpoena shield is that it answers the wrong question by answering a different question. The relevant question for admissibility purposes is not whether the defense received the document through their subpoena. The relevant question is whether the document is what it purports to be: a record generated by the SFPD in connection with the investigation of this collision. If it is, it is admissible under Evidence Code section 1280 regardless of how or through whom it came to the court's attention. A party need not receive a document through a particular channel to authenticate it or to have it admitted. The authentication question and the admissibility question are separate from the question of who received what through which discovery mechanism.

But the court, operating in real time, heard the objection through the lens of the accumulated false representations. Navaratnasingham had represented, at the February 18 hearing, that the defense had not received certain materials. She had used the "did not receive" framing consistently throughout the pretrial period. Now she was using the "not in our subpoena" framing, which was the subpoena-specific version of the same pattern: frame possession as non-receipt to create the impression that the document is unverifiable. The court heard this in context of everything that had gone before. "It is not evidence. It is not in the report." The supplemental narrative that would have corroborated Rosario's account was excluded.

Business and Professions Code section 6068(d) states that it is the duty of an attorney "never to seek to mislead the judge or any judicial officer by an artifice or false statement of fact or law." The "subpoena shield" is an artifice in the precise statutory sense: a device constructed to mislead, to create an impression that could not survive direct examination of the underlying facts. Minutes later, the same attorney would admit that the body-worn camera footage had been "provided by Dolan," collapsing the "not received" narrative that the subpoena shield had been designed to reinforce.

---

### III. The Admission Minutes Later: "Provided by Dolan"

The court's question about the body-worn camera footage on the defense's exhibit list was a routine case management question. The court wanted to know the source of the footage the defense intended to present. The exchange was brief. At RT 7, page 214, lines 13-15, Navaratnasingham answered: "Sure. They were provided by Dolan. He has had them for whatever, sure."

Unpack every word of that response. "Sure." This is not the phrasing of someone revealing information carefully. This is the casual acknowledgment of a settled, recognized fact within the defense team. The body-worn camera footage came from Dolan. That was known. That had always been known. "They were provided by Dolan." Past tense. Established fact. Not "we believe they may have originated from Dolan," not "based on our investigation, it appears," not any of the careful hedging language that appears when a lawyer is establishing an uncertain factual claim. "Were provided." They know. "He has had them for whatever, sure." He, Rosario. Has had. Past continuous. Duration. And "for whatever, sure": dismissive familiarity. The footage from Dolan, in Rosario's possession, for some period of time, who cares, yes.

Jessup had testified at pages 139-140 of the hearing transcript that the Dolan production to defense counsel included body-worn camera footage, the 911 audio, and the CAD log as a unified package, served together in the same discovery response, used together in the deposition of the defendant. Navaratnasingham had said at the February 18 hearing: "I am representing to this Court that we did not have those two items until the day of the MSC." Those two items: the 911 call and the body-worn camera footage. Now, in the middle of trial, with the jury in the box: "They were provided by Dolan."

If the body-worn camera footage was provided by Dolan, then everything Jessup said about the unified production is confirmed. If the body-worn camera footage was provided by Dolan, and the 911 call was provided in the same production package as Jessup testified, then the 911 call was also in the defense's possession through the same channel. "News to me" was false. "Did not receive until MSC" was false. Every exclusionary ruling, every continuance denial, every procedural constraint imposed on Rosario's trial preparation: the product of a fraud on the court that had now confessed itself in open court, in front of the judge, on the record.

---

### IV. The Court's Ruling and Its Foundation

The court excluded the supplemental report on the basis of the objection. The ruling was made in reliance on the representations the defense had made, at the hearing and through the MILs, about the provenance and status of the police records. The court did not have the analytical framework, in real time, to connect the "not in our subpoena" objection to the Jessup testimony, the Compex subpoena, and the "provided by Dolan" admission that would follow minutes later. The court was doing what courts do: applying the law to the facts as they appeared from the representations of counsel.

This is how fraud on the court operates in practice. It does not require the court to be corrupt. It requires only that the court extend to officers of the court the ordinary, necessary trust that makes the adversary system function. That trust, betrayed by a systematic pattern of false representations, produced a series of rulings that stripped Rosario of the evidence he needed to present his case. The supplemental report was excluded. The 911 recording was deferred. The body-worn camera footage was presented on the defense's terms, curated and edited to eight or nine minutes. The unified evidentiary picture that would have anchored liability was never assembled for the jury.

This is Link 6 in the Reliance Matrix: false representation (subpoena shield, creating the impression that the supplemental report's provenance was uncertain) → court ruling (exclusion, "it is not evidence") → deprivation (Rosario cannot corroborate his account with the supplemental narrative). The causal chain from the fraud to the harm is direct and documented. The [Trial Day 7 transcript](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/05-TRANSCRIPTS/REPORTER-TRANSCRIPTS/Trial-Day-07-April-16-2025.pdf) and the [EXHIBIT-TRIAL-DAY-7-ADMISSION](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/04-EXHIBITS/EXHIBIT-TRIAL-DAY-7-ADMISSION.pdf) preserve both the objection and the subsequent admission. The transcript speaks. The fraud had already done its work. What remained was to build the record that would expose it.
