# CHAPTER 13: 9-3 — The Minimum Majority

---

## Narrative

On April 23, 2025, the jury returned its verdict. Nine to three. Defense prevails. The finding on liability: the defendant was not negligent, or his negligence was not a substantial factor in causing harm to Rosario. Nine jurors voted for that conclusion. Three did not. Code of Civil Procedure section 618 requires a three-fourths majority for a civil verdict: nine of twelve. The defense had prevailed by exactly the statutory minimum. One juror, persuaded differently on any single issue, would have produced a hung jury or a plaintiff's verdict.

The nine-to-three number is not merely a statistical observation. It is an evidentiary statement. It says that the trial produced a divided result on a closely contested record. It says that whatever the jury saw and heard, it was not persuasive enough to achieve consensus. In a case where the defendant had authenticated his own admission that he had made contact with the pedestrian, where the physical evidence included a scene captured by five body-worn cameras, where a formal 911 recording existed from the moment of the incident, a nine-to-three verdict is not the verdict of a jury that had seen and evaluated the full evidentiary picture. It is the verdict of a jury that saw a portion of it.

The jury had asked for the governing Vehicle Code provisions and been told it could not consider them. The 911 recording had been deferred and presented out of sequence. The supplemental report had been excluded. The body-worn camera footage had been presented by the defense in a curated, eight-or-nine-minute edit. The unified evidentiary picture that the Dolan production made possible had been systematically fragmented by seven distinct false representations, each one producing a specific court ruling that foreclosed a specific avenue of proof.

Three jurors voted for the plaintiff. One of the three, having seen the unified evidentiary picture without the fragmentation, without the excluded supplemental report, with the governing statutory duty applied, with the 911 recording heard in context at the beginning of the liability case rather than belatedly at the end, might have persuaded one of the nine. The verdict of nine to three is the gap between what the jury saw and what the jury should have seen. That gap is the measure of the harm. The [appeal](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/tree/main/01-APPEAL), the [equity action](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/02-CASE-CGC-25-631801/01-Second-Amended-Complaint.pdf), and the [fraud claim](https://github.com/soltrinox/CSAA-ABDELHALIM-WONG-PSA-CSK/blob/main/03-CASE-CGC-25-631802/01-First-Amended-Complaint.pdf) are the instruments by which that gap will be addressed.

---

### I. The Verdict: Nine to Three

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 618, a verdict in a civil trial may be rendered by three-fourths of the jury. In a twelve-person jury, three-fourths is nine. A verdict of nine to three is the minimum majority. It is the threshold at which a civil verdict can stand. Below nine, the jury is hung. Above nine, the margin increases. At nine exactly, the result is final but barely so: one fewer vote for the prevailing side, and the outcome is different.

The significance of this minimum majority is analytical. A verdict rendered by twelve to zero reflects a jury that was persuaded unanimously. A verdict of ten to two reflects a jury that reached a strong consensus with limited dissent. A verdict of nine to three reflects a jury that was divided, with twenty-five percent of its members unconvinced by the evidence the majority found persuasive. In a case as factually contested as this one, a nine-to-three verdict tells us that the evidence was not overwhelming in either direction. The three dissenting jurors were not unreasonable. They had seen the same evidence as the nine and reached a different conclusion. The question is whether the evidence they saw was the evidence that should have been before them.

The defense verdict was not the product of overwhelming evidence. It was the product of a proceeding in which the plaintiff's evidentiary case had been systematically disrupted: the 911 recording deferred, the supplemental report excluded, the governing statutory duty withheld from the jury's deliberation, the body-worn camera footage presented on the defense's terms. Against this disrupted evidentiary presentation, the defense expert's biomechanical analysis of the forces involved in the collision could fill the gaps. If the jury does not hear the governing statutory duty, and does not hear the corroborating documentary evidence, the expert's opinion has less to overcome. Nine to three.

---

### II. What the Jury Never Received

The evidentiary record of this case, as it actually exists in the certified transcript and the documents in the case repository, contains the following elements that the jury either never received or received in a form that stripped them of their full evidentiary force:

The 911 recording should have anchored the liability case; instead it arrived late, out of sequence. The supplemental report was excluded on the "subpoena shield" objection. The body-worn camera footage was presented in an eight-or-nine-minute defense-curated edit; Navaratnasingham admitted it was "provided by Dolan." The jury asked for Vehicle Code sections 22107 and 21804(a); the court refused. Each item is central to liability. Together they would have established: contact (911 admission), statutory violation (Veh. Code), and corroboration (footage, report). Without them, or with them fragmented, the jury's task was materially different.

---

### III. The Structural Argument: Extrinsic Fraud

The verdict of nine to three was not the product of a fair adversary hearing. It was the product of a proceeding in which the adversary system had been corrupted at its foundation. Not by witness perjury of the kind that juries are equipped to evaluate by weighing credibility. By attorney fraud directed at the court, manipulating the procedural and evidentiary framework within which the jury would deliberate.

Extrinsic fraud is fraud that prevents a party from having a fair adversary hearing on the merits. It is distinguished from intrinsic fraud, which is fraud within the proceeding that the adversary system is designed to expose and that the jury evaluates. Intrinsic fraud is a witness lying on the stand; the adversary process has mechanisms for that: cross-examination, impeachment, counter-evidence. Extrinsic fraud is a party manipulating the procedural framework of the proceeding so that the adversary process itself cannot function correctly. The adversary process cannot correct extrinsic fraud because the fraud operates on the structure of the process itself.

The false representations made to the court in this case are extrinsic fraud in the technical legal sense. They were not misrepresentations to the jury for the jury to evaluate. They were misrepresentations to the judge, shaping the judge's rulings on continuances, evidentiary motions, and jury instructions. The jury never knew about the false representations. The jury could not evaluate them. The jury deliberated within a procedural and evidentiary framework that had been corrupted by the fraud before the jury was ever empaneled.

Under Kulchar v. Kulchar (1969) 1 Cal.3d 467, equity will set aside a final judgment where extrinsic fraud prevented a party from having a fair adversary hearing on the merits. The equity action in CGC-25-631801 is the procedural vehicle for that remedy. The nine-to-three verdict is the evidence of prejudice: the trial produced a minimum-majority verdict in a case where, had the plaintiff been able to present the full evidentiary picture without the disruption the fraud caused, the result could have been different.

---

### IV. The Fight Is Not Over

The nine-to-three verdict is the end of the trial. It is not the end of the litigation. The appeal, the equity action, and the fraud claim are three independent proceedings, each with its own legal theory, decision-maker, and remedy. The verdict is not final in the sense of being immune from challenge. It is the current state of a record that is still being built.

The appeal challenges the instructional error on independent grounds that do not require the court of appeal to reach the fraud issues. If the appeal succeeds, the case is reversed and remanded for a new trial, at which the full evidentiary picture can be presented without the corruptions the fraud introduced.

The equity action challenges the verdict directly on the grounds of extrinsic fraud. If the equity action succeeds, the judgment is vacated and the case is retried on the fair record that the extrinsic fraud prevented.

The fraud action seeks compensation for the harms caused by the first lie: the denial letter, the premature conclusion of the investigation, and the years of insurance bad faith that followed from that original false determination.

Nine to three. The minimum civil majority. The verdict stands today. The three proceedings now underway will determine whether it stands tomorrow.
