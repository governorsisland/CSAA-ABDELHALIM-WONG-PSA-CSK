The duty of candor is the foundational assumption on which the adversary system rests. *In re Silverton* (2005) 36 Cal.4th 81, 91 holds that an attorney's duty of candor to the tribunal is fundamental; its breach undermines the integrity of the judicial process. The arbiter trusts that attorneys present their cases within the constraints of truthfulness. That trust is structural necessity. When it is exploited, the system cannot self-correct in real time.

When that trust is exploited, the system does not self-correct. The court cannot know in real time that the representation it has accepted is false. The ruling it makes on the basis of the false representation carries the full authority of a judicial order. The party against whom the ruling was made has no procedural mechanism for immediate correction. The harm flows downstream from the ruling, shaping the trial that follows and the verdict that results. The party harmed is left to seek post-trial remedies -- appeal, equitable vacatur -- from courts that must evaluate the fraud in the record they were not present to observe. This is why extrinsic fraud is the gravest species of judicial corruption. It corrupts the judicial process from within, using the system's own mechanisms of trust against the party the system was designed to protect.

### I. The Foundational Obligation

Business and Professions Code section 6068(d) is one of the oldest provisions of California attorney ethics law. It imposes on every attorney the duty to "employ, for the purpose of maintaining the causes confided to him or her those means only as are consistent with truth, and never to seek to mislead the judge or any judicial officer by an artifice or false statement of fact or law." The statutory language is worth parsing carefully.

"Means only as are consistent with truth": not merely a prohibition on lying, but an affirmative requirement that the attorney's means, the tactics, the arguments, the representations, must be consistent with truth. An argument that is technically accurate but designed to create a false impression violates this standard. An objection that is technically limited to one channel of receipt but designed to create the impression of complete non-possession is an artifice within the meaning of the statute.

"Never to seek to mislead the judge... by an artifice": the prohibition on artifice is independent of the prohibition on false statements. An artifice is a device or stratagem designed to mislead without technically lying. The "subpoena shield" is an artifice: an objection framed in terms of what the defense received through its own subpoena, designed to create the impression that the materials had not been received through any channel, when in fact they had been received through the Dolan production. This is the statutory definition of the conduct the statute prohibits.

California Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 3.3(a)(1) provides that a lawyer shall not knowingly make a false statement of fact or law to a tribunal. "Knowingly" is defined in the Rules to include actual knowledge and, under some circumstances, knowledge that can be inferred from the circumstances. A lawyer who makes a representation about the discovery record in a case the lawyer has been managing for twenty months without reviewing the file has not acted "without knowledge"; the knowledge is imputed from the circumstances of the representation.

### II. The Presumption of Candor and Its Exploitation

The presumption of candor is the judicial system's operating assumption that attorneys appearing before the court are telling the truth. It is not a legal presumption in the formal evidentiary sense; it is a functional presumption embedded in the institutional design of the adversary system. Courts extend to attorneys the trust that their professional obligations warrant. When an attorney represents to the court that the defense did not receive a recording until the day of the MSC, the court accepts that representation as the baseline factual understanding for the ruling that follows.

Exploitation of the presumption of candor is the mechanism through which extrinsic fraud operates. The false representations at the February 18 hearing worked because the court extended to Navaratnasingham the trust that her professional obligation warranted. "I see. Okay." Three words that capture the functioning of the presumption: the court received the representation, recognized it as a statement from an officer of the court, and accepted it. The presumption did exactly what it was designed to do. The problem was that the representation it accepted was false.

The court was not complicit in the fraud. The court was the victim of it. The court became, as courts become when the presumption of candor is exploited, an unwitting instrument of the fraud: its authority deployed in service of the false representation, its rulings giving legal force to the factual predicate that the false representation had established. The continuance denial carried the full authority of a judicial order. The evidentiary rulings carried the full authority of judicial orders. They were made in good faith. They were made in reliance on a false predicate. The harm they caused was real.

### III. Kulchar and the Equity Remedy

Kulchar v. Kulchar (1969) 1 Cal.3d 467 established the California rule that a court of equity may set aside a final judgment obtained through extrinsic fraud. The case involved a divorce judgment obtained through the failure to disclose material information to the court. The California Supreme Court held that equity would intervene to vacate a judgment where the prevailing party had engaged in extrinsic fraud that prevented the losing party from having a fair adversary hearing on the merits. The principle has been extended and refined in subsequent cases, but the core rule remains: extrinsic fraud is a ground for equitable vacatur of a final judgment.

The equity action in CGC-25-631801 is the procedural vehicle for that remedy. The Kulchar doctrine requires proof of three elements: (1) the representor had knowledge of the falsity of the representation; (2) the false representation was made to the court; and (3) the court relied on the representation in making rulings that deprived the other party of a fair hearing. Each element is documented in the certified transcript and the supporting documents.

Knowledge: Navaratnasingham's "provided by Dolan" admission, combined with the evidence that PSA had curated the body-worn camera footage for trial presentation, establishes that PSA possessed the materials and that Navaratnasingham had knowledge of their Dolan provenance. The subsequent admission proves the prior representations were false; the knowledge element follows from the possession.

False representation to the court: the hearing transcript pages 141-165 document the representations. They were made in open court, in the presence of the judge, in the context of a hearing on Rosario's continuance motion.

Reliance and deprivation: the court's rulings following each representation document the reliance. The continuance denial at hearing transcript page 181. The evidentiary rulings at trial. The jury instruction refusal. Each ruling flowed from the false factual predicate the representations had established. Rosario was deprived of the fair adversary hearing on the merits that the proceeding should have provided.

The remedy: vacatur of the judgment and a new trial on a fair record. The duty of candor was betrayed. Equity will restore what the betrayal took. Officium nemini delegare potest: the duty cannot be delegated. The attorneys who violated it bear the consequences. The plaintiff who paid the price will be made whole.
