# CHAPTER 5: January 17, 2025 — Nineteen Motions in Limine

---

## Narrative

Ten days before trial, the defense filed nineteen motions. Two of them would reshape the case. MIL No. 6 sought to exclude any "recorded materials, photographs, or documents offered by Plaintiff that were not produced in discovery," including "audio of a 911 call purported to be related to the incident." MIL No. 17, signed by Navaratnasingham and Robert K. Phillips, targeted the 911 call recording specifically. It claimed the materials were "not produced during discovery, rather produced at the eleventh hour prior to trial." It claimed the defendant had "no idea where this 911 call log or audio recording came from and who created it." It claimed the defendant had "no idea who created the log and recording, how they were maintained, how Plaintiff got them, or the identity of the custodian." Every claim was false. The materials had been produced to defense counsel during discovery. The defense had received them from the Dolan Law Firm. The defense had had them for nearly two years. Why would a defense team with the evidence in their file claim it was unknown? Because the first lie required the second. If the defense acknowledged possession, the question would follow: how could CSAA have "concluded" a proper investigation twenty-one days after the collision, before the recording even existed as a recoverable artifact? The second lie protected the first. The motions were granted. The exclusionary framework was in place. The stage was set for a trial in which the most powerful evidence of liability would be suppressed before a single witness was sworn.

### I. January 17, 2025: The Day the Trap Was Set

Ten days before the scheduled trial date, on January 17, 2025, Phillips, Spallas & Angstadt filed a comprehensive set of pretrial motions on behalf of Abdelhalim. Nineteen Motions in Limine, a Global Statement of Facts, and a supporting Declaration of Priya Navaratnasingham. Each of the nineteen motions incorporated Navaratnasingham's declaration by reference. The declaration thus became the evidentiary backbone for every exclusionary motion PSA filed. If the declaration was false, the motions premised on it were false. If the false premises were accepted by the court, every resulting ruling was the product of false pretenses. The motions were signed by counsel under penalty of perjury, as required under the applicable rules. They became part of the official court record.

Motions in limine shape the evidentiary landscape before witnesses are sworn. They ask the court to rule in advance on admissibility. Under Evidence Code section 352, the court may exclude evidence when its probative value is substantially outweighed by the probability that its admission will create undue prejudice, confuse the issues, or mislead the jury. When the factual predicates of a motion are false, the ruling procured is not the product of the adversary system functioning properly. It is the product of a court being deceived.

The January 17 filing date is significant. It placed the false representations on the record ten days before trial. It meant that when the February 18, 2025 pretrial hearing occurred, a month before trial, the court would already have before it the filed MIL motions asserting that the 911 recording was "not produced in discovery" and that the defendant had "no idea" where it came from. Those filed motions created a context in which Navaratnasingham's hearing-room representations would be received not as startling new claims but as consistent with what was already in the record. The court had already seen the MIL filings. The "news to me" claim at the hearing was the verbal reinforcement of a written position already established in the court's file.

### II. MIL No. 6 — The Architecture of the Blanket Exclusion

Motion in Limine No. 6 established the broad framework. It sought to exclude from evidence any "recorded materials, photographs, or documents offered by Plaintiff that were not produced in discovery." This is, on its face, an unobjectionable standard for evidence admission: parties should not be permitted to introduce evidence at trial that they withheld during discovery. Discovery exists precisely to prevent surprise, to allow each party to evaluate and prepare responses to the evidence the opposing party intends to use. The baseline principle is sound.

The problem is the factual predicate. The motion premised the exclusionary request on the claim that the plaintiff's materials, including specifically "audio of a 911 call purported to be related to the incident," had not been produced in discovery. That premise was false. The materials had been produced in discovery. Jessup had produced them to prior defense counsel. The production was documented. The materials had been used in depositions. They were not materials produced for the first time on the eve of trial. They were materials that had been in the case record since 2022.

The court, upon receiving MIL No. 6, was being asked to apply a legitimate legal principle to a false factual predicate. The court accepted the premise. The ruling created a blanket framework: anything the court determined had not been properly produced in discovery would be excluded. This framework, premised on the false claim about production, would be used throughout the trial to manage Rosario's attempts to introduce the evidentiary record he had built from the Dolan production. Every time he tried to offer one of these materials, the motion in limine framework would be invoked. The gatekeeping was not ad hoc. It was systematic, pre-set, and based on false representations.

### III. MIL No. 17 — The Surgical Strike Against the Party Admission

Motion in Limine No. 17 was targeted. Where MIL No. 6 created a blanket framework, MIL No. 17 went directly after the most dangerous piece of evidence: the 911 recording containing Abdelhalim's party admission. The motion was signed by Navaratnasingham and Robert K. Phillips. It was backed by Navaratnasingham's global declaration. And it rested on three specific factual predicates, each false, each documented in the record as false.

First false predicate: the materials were "not produced during discovery, rather produced at the eleventh hour prior to trial." This is directly contradicted by Jessup's testimony that the materials were "produced to defense counsel" and "served on them in the production for discovery" and "utilized in the deposition of defendants." The materials were not produced at the eleventh hour. They had been in the case since 2022.

Second false predicate: "Defendant has no idea where this 911 call log or audio recording came from and who created it." This is directly contradicted by the Compex subpoena of February 1, 2024, in which Reyna specifically requested the 911 call by name from the SFPD. A person who has no idea where a 911 call came from does not issue a formal subpoena to the police department specifically requesting that 911 call by name. The subpoena proves knowledge. The motion denied knowledge. The contradiction is in the record.

Third false predicate: "Defendant has no idea who created the log and recording, how they were maintained, how Plaintiff got them, or the identity of the custodian." This is false on multiple levels. The San Francisco Police Department is the custodian of 911 recordings. That is publicly known. The SFPD maintains these records pursuant to its standard document retention policies. The identity of the custodian is not a mystery. Any attorney who has ever requested public records from the SFPD, as Reyna demonstrably had through the Compex subpoena, knows that the SFPD is the custodian. The claim of ignorance about the custodian was, in the context of an attorney who had just issued a formal subpoena to the SFPD requesting that very recording, implausible to the point of being farcical.

### IV. The Institutional Logic: Why the Second Lie Was Required

Why would a defense team with the evidence in their file claim it was unknown? The answer flows from the structure of the two lies. The first lie was CSAA's denial letter. The second lie was required to protect it. If the defense acknowledged that the 911 recording was in their file, had been produced during discovery, and had been in their possession for nearly two years, the question would follow: how did CSAA "conclude" its investigation in twenty-one days? The answer is that CSAA had not obtained the recording. Acknowledging possession would have exposed the first fraud. The second lie was structurally required. By claiming the recording was "unknown," the defense created a tactical fog around the evidence that would demonstrate the falsity of the denial letter. The motions in limine were the formal mechanism through which the fog was deployed.

The motions were granted. The exclusionary framework was in place. Rosario would enter the courtroom without a timely presentation of the 911 recording, without the supplemental police report, and without the governing statutory framework he had requested. The January 17 filings were the opening move of the second lie.
