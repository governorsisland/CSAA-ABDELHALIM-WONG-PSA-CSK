## PART I: BEFORE THE COLLISION

### Mr. Rosario's Employment History and Background

Franciscus Dylan Rosario was a senior AI systems architect whose W-2 compensation in the years before the collision ranged between $550,000 and $1,000,000 per year. He was the lead architect for companies including Rowzzy Co, where employees such as Robert Bloodgood and Benjamin Giler worked with him.

### Subhi Abdelhalim and Sonoma Liquor

Subhi Abdelhalim was born on July 13, 1955, in Palestine, where he completed high school before emigrating to the United States. By the date of the collision he was sixty-five years old. He had lived at 180 Allen Drive in San Bruno, California since 1987 with his wife Saida and their four children: Fadi, Arfat, Sami, and Amy. None of his children worked at the store or lived in the household at the time.

He owned and operated Sonoma Liquor Company, a corner store business located at 65 Sixth Street in San Francisco's South of Market district, one block east of Fifth Street, at the corner of Sixth and Jesse. The building itself was held under the entity Halim Brothers, a tax-identification structure shared with his brother, though Abdelhalim was the primary operator of the business. His duties were total: opening the store, working as cashier, stocking the floor, and driving to wholesalers in Brisbane when deliveries fell short. He held a California driver's license continuously since 1980. It had never been suspended. He carried no corrective lens restriction. By his own testimony at deposition, he neither smoked nor drank.

His vehicle was a 2018 Ford Transit cargo van, purchased used, personally owned and registered in his name, not in the business name. He drove it primarily for personal transportation and for store supply runs to wholesalers in Brisbane when needed. No one else drove it. He described the van as "my baby." On the morning of February 4, 2021, the van was completely empty, no cargo, no hand truck, no merchandise.

His routine that morning was fixed. He woke at 5:00 a.m., showered, arrived at the store by 5:30 to open, and stayed until relief workers arrived. On February 4, he had stayed past the arrival of help, "hanging around a little bit," as he described it at deposition, and departed around ten minutes to three in the afternoon. He parked, as was his custom when arriving early, in Jesse Alley: a one-way alleyway running between Fifth and Sixth Streets that offered free, unmetered parking. From Jesse Alley, his route home to San Bruno took him west to Fifth Street and Mission, south on Fifth toward the I-80 freeway onramp accessible via Sixth Street, with a right turn onto Tehama to cut over.

He was insured under a commercial vehicle liability policy through CSAA Insurance Exchange (AAA) with a $500,000 policy limit. CSAA funded, directed, and controlled the defense from inception through trial.

## PART II: FEBRUARY 4, 2021, THE DAY OF THE COLLISION

### The Afternoon Leading to Impact

At 2:18 PM, Frank Rosario rented a Lime electric scooter at Fifth and Mission. At 2:19 PM, the electric motor malfunctioned. He continued using it as a non-motorized kick scooter, pushing it west along Fifth Street. At 2:39 PM, he arrived at Fifth and Tehama, walking westbound and pushing the malfunctioning scooter along the pedestrian crosswalk.

Abdelhalim had left Sonoma Liquor approximately ten minutes to three that same afternoon. He exited Jesse Alley, turned left onto Mission, waited at the light at Fifth and Mission, then proceeded southbound on Fifth Street in the right lane. After crossing Howard Street, he approached Tehama, a one-way alley running westbound; and began his right turn. He later testified he had his turn signal on, was traveling at approximately five miles per hour, and that the alley ahead appeared clear before he completed the turn. He admitted at deposition that he did not see Rosario before the collision.

### The Impact

At 2:40 PM, Abdelhalim ignored a double yield sign, crossed the bicycle lane, and struck Rosario from behind in the crosswalk. (CVC § 21950(a).) Rosario did not see the vehicle before impact. He sustained a traumatic brain injury, fractured ribs, and spinal damage. Justin Rosemond, an eyewitness at the scene, saw the collision. After impact, as Rosario lay on the ground, he regained enough awareness to look up and saw the van paused with Abdelhalim looking out the passenger-side window at him. Abdelhalim then slowly moved the vehicle down the street to conceal it.

### 2:41 PM: Driver Flees

At 2:41 PM, Abdelhalim fled the scene without calling 911 or providing assistance. (CVC § 20001(a).)

### 2:42 PM: Passerby Confirms Hit-and-Run

At 2:42 PM, an unknown passerby driving down Fifth Street observed Rosario alone on the sidewalk with no vehicle in sight and no one providing aid. Hit-and-run confirmed. (CVC § 20001(c).)

### 2:44 PM: Rosario Calls Sister

At 2:44 PM, Rosario called his sister Emily.

### 2:45 PM: Rosario Crawls to Corner

At 2:45 PM, Rosario crawled to the corner. The driver was still absent.

### 2:49 PM: Driver Returns

At 2:49 PM, Abdelhalim returned to the scene with the vehicle. (Penal Code § 20001(b)(2).)

### 2:50 PM: Photograph Taken

At 2:50 PM, Rosario took a photograph of Abdelhalim as he returned.

### 2:51 PM: Bribery Attempt

At 2:51 PM, Abdelhalim offered Rosario a five-dollar bill. Rosario refused and told him the evidence would lead to his arrest. (Penal Code § 67.5.)

### 2:53 PM: 911 Call and Confession

At 2:53 PM, Abdelhalim called 911 after repeated demands. During the call, he admitted hitting the victim; an excited utterance under Evidence Code section 1240. Within the same call, he changed his story and fabricated a narrative that Rosario had fallen while riding the scooter. (Evidence Code § 1240; Penal Code § 118.)

### 2:54 PM: Third-Party 911 Call

At 2:54 PM, a separate, unknown passerby called 911. That caller had observed the scene a few minutes earlier: Rosario on the sidewalk with no vehicle in sight and no one helping. The caller reported a hit-and-run and stated with certainty there was "no van" and nobody providing assistance.

### 2:55 PM: First Responders Dispatched

At 2:55 PM, first responders were dispatched.

### 2:58 PM: EMTs Arrive: Driver Denies

At 2:58 PM, EMTs from the fire department arrived. Abdelhalim immediately denied hitting the victim, contradicting his 911 admission. He claimed Rosario had fallen off the scooter. (Penal Code § 148.5(a).)

### 3:00 PM: Officer Thompson Records Eyewitness

At 3:00 PM, Officer Thompson recorded eyewitness testimony. Justin Rosemond reported the collision to the responding officers. Officer Thompson's body-worn camera recorded Rosemond stating that the guy in the van hit the guy laying down on the ground.

### 3:01 PM: Officer Simmons Interrogates Rosario; Driver Lies to Officer DeLeon

At 3:01 PM, Officer Simmons approached Rosario, who was receiving emergency medical treatment, and began an interview. Rosario had a traumatic brain injury, five posterior rib fractures, and spinal damage. He was in extremis. Simmons asked a leading question: "How fast were you going?" The question assumed Rosario had been operating the scooter. Rosario, brain-damaged and in agony, responded that the vehicles only go five miles per hour; a statement about the scooter's mechanical capability, not an admission of operation. The body-worn cameras captured the question and the answer. Nowhere on any body-worn camera does Rosario state that he was riding or operating the scooter at the time of the collision. The officers inferred operation from a statement about top speed and attributed this inference to Rosario as a voluntary admission in the police report.

Simultaneously, Abdelhalim told Officer DeLeon he had no contact with and no involvement in the incident. DeLeon was not a neutral investigator. The body-worn camera footage shows DeLeon's interaction with Abdelhalim demonstrated familiarity. DeLeon was a patron of Sonoma Liquor, the store Abdelhalim owned and operated at the corner of Sixth and Jesse in the South of Market neighborhood that DeLeon patrolled. The body-worn cameras captured a casual tone between the two men inconsistent with a neutral police investigation of a serious pedestrian collision. DeLeon accepted Abdelhalim's account without challenge, without confronting him with the 911 confession already in the dispatch system, without asking why a man claiming "no contact" had called 911 to report an injured person, and without reconciling the defendant's denial with the eyewitness account captured on Officer Thompson's camera.

### The Loaded Question and the Scooter Inference

The body-worn camera recordings establish that officers asked Rosario "how fast were you going" while he was receiving emergency medical treatment; a loaded question that presupposed scooter operation. Rosario's response that the scooter "only goes five miles per hour" was a statement about the device's speed limit, not an admission of operation. The officers extracted this statement from a brain-injured man in extremis and converted it in the police report into an inference that Rosario was operating the scooter illegally. No body-worn camera footage shows Rosario stating he was riding the scooter. No eyewitness reported him riding. Justin Rosemond described a van hitting a pedestrian. The second 911 caller described a person down on the sidewalk, not a scooter accident. The 911 confession from the driver described hitting a person, not colliding with a scooter. Every piece of primary evidence described a pedestrian collision.

### SFPD Institutional Context: Scooter Enforcement Battles

In 2021, the SFPD was engaged in ongoing institutional conflict with electric scooter companies over operation and safety on city streets. The department had been handling complaints about scooter riders violating traffic regulations. This institutional context predisposed officers to associate a scooter at an accident scene with illegal scooter operation, regardless of the evidence. At Fifth and Tehama, the officers saw a scooter, inferred illegal operation, and wrote a scooter-accident report despite the absence of any evidence that Rosario had been riding rather than walking.

### DeLeon's Relationship with the Defendant

The body-worn camera footage establishes that Officer DeLeon, the officer who took Abdelhalim's statement, had a pre-existing familiarity with the defendant. Abdelhalim owned and operated Sonoma Liquor at the corner of Sixth and Jesse, within DeLeon's patrol area. The body-worn cameras captured a tone and casualness between DeLeon and Abdelhalim inconsistent with a neutral investigation of a serious pedestrian collision. DeLeon was a patron of Sonoma Liquor. The body-worn camera recordings also captured a casualness among the officers toward the injured plaintiff, including laughter at the scene, that is inconsistent with the severity of the injuries and the felony nature of the incident.

### Officer Fernandez as Strawman Author

The police report was authored by Officer David Fernandez, a junior officer who did not witness the collision, arrived by dispatch after the fact, and had "very little independent recollection of the event." The more experienced officers who had interacted with Abdelhalim and whose cameras had captured the eyewitness testimony did not write the report. DeLeon, who had the personal relationship with the defendant and was the primary conduit for the defendant's fabricated account, did not sign the report. Fernandez relied on synopses from colleagues and later acknowledged at deposition he was "mostly just going off the police report"; the report he himself had authored. The assignment of a junior officer as the named author insulated the more experienced officers from accountability for the fabricated narrative they had constructed.

### 3:12 PM: Fabricated Police Narrative

At 3:12 PM, the SFPD claimed to have obtained a verbal confession from Rosario admitting to breaking motor vehicle traffic laws. This alleged confession is entirely absent from all five body-worn camera recordings. The police submitted an official incident report assigning fault to Rosario. The report omitted: (1) Justin Rosemond's eyewitness account captured on Officer Thompson's body-worn camera; (2) the driver's 911 confession "I barely hit him" in the dispatch system; (3) the five-dollar bribery attempt (Penal Code section 67.5); (4) the nine-minute hit-and-run (Vehicle Code section 20001(a)); (5) the anonymous passerby's corroborating 911 call confirming "no van" and nobody helping; (6) the fracture pattern of five posterior rib fractures, biomechanically inconsistent with a scooter fall and consistent only with rear vehicle impact. The report adopted the defendant's narrative wholesale while omitting every piece of evidence that contradicted it. The omission of the eyewitness account is independently actionable under California Government Code sections 815.2 and 820, as a public employee act or omission within the scope of employment that proximately caused injury.

### The Police-to-Insurance Pipeline

The fabricated police report became the direct foundation for CSAA's February 25, 2021 denial letter. CSAA relied on the Fernandez report without obtaining the body-worn camera footage, the 911 recordings, or the CAD dispatch logs that contradicted it. The police fabrication provided the insurance company with an official government document blaming the victim, which CSAA adopted as the basis for its categorical denial of liability. The link between the police fabrication and the insurance denial is direct and documented: three institutions: the SFPD, the Fernandez report, and CSAA: adopted the same fabricated narrative that originated with the defendant's self-serving account delivered to an officer who was a patron of the defendant's liquor store.

### 3:15 PM: SFPD Releases Driver

At 3:15 PM, despite eyewitness testimony, the driver's 911 confession, evidence of a felony hit-and-run (VC 20001(a)), attempted witness bribery (PC 67.5), false report to police (PC 148.5(a)), and traffic violations (VC 21950(a), 22107), the SFPD allowed Abdelhalim to leave the scene. No arrest. No citation. No vehicle impound. The van was not photographed for impact evidence. The intersection was not photographed for debris patterns. The scene was not secured as a crime scene despite the felony hit-and-run. The probable cause for arrest was established independently by: the 911 confession, the eyewitness testimony, the anonymous passerby's corroboration, and the visible injuries. The failure to arrest, cite, impound, or investigate constitutes a complete dereliction of professional police duty.

### 3:18 PM: Driver Departs; Rosario Transported

By 3:18 PM, Abdelhalim was gone. Rosario was transported by ambulance. Diagnosis: traumatic brain injury, fractured ribs, spinal damage. Benjamin Giler, an associate of Rowzzy, picked Rosario up and took him home. Victoria Brooks Rosario, his then-fiancee, was involved in his care.

### February 4, 2021, Evening: Abdelhalim Calls AAA

On the evening of February 4, 2021, after returning to his home at 180 Allen Drive in San Bruno, Abdelhalim told his wife Saida what had happened. By his own account at deposition, she asked what was wrong; he told her. He then called AAA: CSAA Insurance Exchange. Immediately, to report the incident and obtain a claim number. He received that claim number the same day. An adjuster was subsequently assigned.

This same-evening call is a critical evidentiary fact. It means CSAA received its insured's personal account of the February 4 collision on February 4, 2021. Before any public records request was possible, before any investigation could have begun. The insured described what he characterized as a routine turn during which he heard screaming, stopped as "a good citizen," called 911, and was cleared by police. He denied any contact. CSAA had, from the moment it opened the file, two things: an insured who denied fault and a claimant it had not yet contacted. That foundation was enough. Twenty-one days later, the denial letter arrived.

That same evening, Abdelhalim also prepared handwritten notes recording his account of the incident. He described them at his September 30, 2022 deposition: he had written down his route, the turn onto Tehama, the screaming he heard after completing the turn, his approach to Rosario, his 911 call, and the subsequent police interaction. He read the notes into the deposition record. They state, in his own hand, that he "do not feel any impact to my car." He reviewed these notes "again and again" before his deposition to ensure accuracy. These contemporaneous notes are the earliest written post-incident account from the defendant. Prepared before any attorney retained, before any police report reviewed, and disclosed at deposition nearly twenty months later.

## PART IV: FEBRUARY 25, 2021, THE DENIAL LETTER

### Twenty-One Days

On February 25, 2021, twenty-one days after the collision, CSAA Insurance Exchange mailed Frank Rosario a letter. The letter stated that CSAA had "concluded" its investigation and determined that its insured, Subhi Abdelhalim, was not liable. The word "concluded" carries an implied factual predicate: the investigation is finished because the investigative steps have been performed. A reasonable person receiving that letter from a multi-billion-dollar insurer would understand "concluded" to mean that someone had obtained the police report, requested the 911 recording, reviewed the body-worn camera footage, and examined the dispatch logs. CSAA had done none of these things. Its claim file contained no record of a 911 request, no body-worn camera request, no CAD log request. The "concluded" representation was false when made. This was the first lie. It was institutional. It was pre-litigation. And it set the anchor from which every subsequent fraud would be measured.

### The Logical Impossibility

Under California law, an insurer has a duty to investigate before denying a claim when liability is contested. The 911 recording, body-worn camera footage, and CAD logs were all public records, obtainable through routine written requests. The 911 call contained the driver's own words: "I barely hit him." CSAA had not made those requests. In twenty-one days, before any of these materials had been sought, let alone reviewed, CSAA's adjuster wrote that the company had "concluded" its investigation. The denial letter was unsolicited. Rosario had never communicated with the insurance company before receiving it. The letter was sent by a corporation doing business as CSAA Insurance Exchange, affiliated with AAA. The template-based denial without investigation would later form the basis of a fraud action against CSAA, and the logical impossibility of the defense position would be proven by the original denial letter itself.

### What the Denial Letter Should Have Said

The February 25, 2021 denial letter, signed by CSAA adjuster Sarah Rash, made two affirmative statements of completed fact: "We have concluded our investigation of your claim" and "Based on our investigation, and after carefully reviewing the facts and circumstances of your claim, we have concluded that our insured is not liable for your damages resulting from this loss. Therefore we must decline your claim." Both statements are representations about a past event, a completed investigative process. Under California law, those words created a legal obligation that was unavoidable regardless of which investigative path CSAA had taken.

#### Scenario One: CSAA Had the Records and Still Denied Liability

If CSAA obtained the 911 audio, the body-worn camera footage, and the CAD logs before February 25, 2021, and then concluded the insured was not liable, the denial letter becomes an independent fraud for a different reason: it is facially impossible to hear Abdelhalim say "I barely hit him" on the 911 call and honestly conclude the insured bears no liability for a pedestrian struck in a crosswalk under a double yield sign. But beyond the liability determination itself, California Insurance Code section 790.03(h)(13) required the letter to disclose what was reviewed and why the evidence supported the denial. A claimant is entitled to understand the factual basis for a denial. A legally compliant denial under Scenario One would have identified the specific materials reviewed, tied the denial to actual evidence, and given Rosario a factual basis for evaluating whether to contest the determination. The letter CSAA actually sent did none of those things. It used the word "concluded" as a closing, not as an explanation.

#### Scenario Two: CSAA Did Not Obtain the Records and Still Issued a Denial

This is the scenario the record supports. California law gave CSAA two honest options. The first was a status letter: "We are continuing our investigation. We are in the process of obtaining the police report, dispatch records, and related materials. We are not in a position to make a liability determination at this time." That letter is honest. It does not use the word "concluded." It does not represent a completed process. It preserves CSAA's right to later deny or accept the claim once the actual investigation is performed. The second option was a reservation of rights letter, which acknowledges that investigation is ongoing and no coverage position is being waived. What California law does not permit is issuing a letter that says the investigation is "concluded" and the insured is "not liable" when the foundational records of the incident have never been requested. That formulation is not a denial. It is not a reservation. It is a false statement of completed fact.

#### The Word "Concluded" Was Not Optional Language

CSAA could have written the letter in dozens of ways without committing fraud. It could have said: "We are still investigating." Or: "We have reviewed the materials currently available to us and are unable to determine liability at this time." Or: "Based on our insured's account, we do not believe he was at fault, but we are continuing to gather information." Any of those formulations is legally defensible. None of them represent investigative finality. None of them expose CSAA to a fraud claim based on a false completed-fact statement. The letter CSAA actually sent used "concluded" twice and "carefully reviewing" once. Those three words are the fraud. They affirmatively represent that a process (obtaining records, reviewing them, reaching a conclusion) was performed. That process was never performed. The word "concluded" was not accidental phrasing. It was the instrument of institutional pressure: telling an unrepresented, seriously injured claimant that the most powerful actor in the room had already done its homework and the answer was no. Under the binary trap documented in CGC-25-631802, CSAA had no lawful path to that specific language on February 25, 2021. Either it had the records and the denial was knowing fraud, or it did not have the records and the "concluded" representation was false when made. The letter was legally indefensible the moment Sarah Rash signed it.

### The Statutory Violations

The denial letter did not merely represent a bad business judgment. It violated California law in three independent respects.

California Insurance Code section 790.03(h)(3) makes it an unlawful unfair insurance practice to "fail to adopt and implement reasonable standards for the prompt investigation and processing of claims." A twenty-one-day investigation that never contacted the SFPD, never requested the 911 recording, never obtained the body-worn camera footage, and never pulled the CAD logs is not an investigation under any reasonable standard. It is a template denial dressed up as a conclusion.

California Insurance Code section 790.03(h)(13) independently requires an insurer to promptly provide a reasonable explanation of the basis in the insurance policy for a denial. The February 25 letter provided no such explanation. It stated the investigation was "concluded" and the insured was "not liable" without identifying a single piece of evidence reviewed, a single witness contacted, or a single record obtained. A claimant receiving that letter had no basis to evaluate whether the determination was correct or how to contest it.

The implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, read into every California insurance policy by operation of law under Comunale v. Traders & General Ins. Co. (1958) 50 Cal.2d 654, required CSAA to give at least as much consideration to the claimant's interests as to its own. Issuing a categorical denial before obtaining the foundational evidence of the incident inverted that duty entirely. CSAA placed its financial interest (closing the file) above its obligation to conduct a genuine inquiry. Under Wilson v. 21st Century Ins. Co. (2007) 42 Cal.4th 713, bad faith is established where the insurer fails to investigate and denies on an inadequate factual record.

### The Legal Precedent: A Century of Courts Condemning This Exact Conduct

CSAA's twenty-one-day denial was not a novel form of institutional misconduct. California courts have been identifying, condemning, and punishing precisely this behavior for more than half a century. The precedent is not ambiguous. It is not evolving. It is settled, explicit, and devastating to any insurer that denies a claim without first conducting a genuine investigation.

In Gruenberg v. Aetna Ins. Co. (1973) 9 Cal.3d 566, the California Supreme Court first declared that an insurer's bad faith refusal to pay policy benefits is not merely a breach of contract but a tort, an independent civil wrong carrying the full weight of tort damages, including emotional distress and punitive damages. Before Gruenberg, an insurer that wrongfully denied a claim faced only contract remedies: the amount owed under the policy plus interest. The damages were calculable, predictable, and manageable. An insurer could deny ten claims, get caught on three, pay the policy amount on those three, and still profit on the seven that gave up. Gruenberg changed the equation. The Supreme Court recognized what the insurance industry had known for decades: that the power imbalance between a multi-billion-dollar insurer and an individual claimant was so extreme that contract remedies were insufficient to deter abuse. Only tort liability, with its potential for emotional distress damages and punitive awards, could make the calculus of wrongful denial unprofitable.

In Egan v. Mutual of Omaha Ins. Co. (1979) 24 Cal.3d 809, the Supreme Court drove the principle deeper. Egan involved a disability insurer that denied benefits without adequately investigating the claim. The Court held, at page 819, that an insurer cannot reasonably deny a claim when it has failed to investigate the facts upon which its denial is based. The holding is directly applicable to CSAA's conduct: an insurer that issues a categorical denial in twenty-one days without requesting the 911 recording, the body-worn camera footage, or the dispatch logs has failed to investigate the facts upon which its denial is based. The denial is unreasonable as a matter of law. Egan established that the duty to investigate is not aspirational. It is a prerequisite to denial. An insurer that skips the investigation and proceeds directly to denial has committed bad faith regardless of whether the claim would have been meritorious.

In Neal v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1978) 21 Cal.3d 910, the Supreme Court addressed the question of punitive damages in the insurance bad faith context. The Court held that when an insurer's conduct reflects a "conscious disregard" of the insured's or claimant's rights, punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294 are available. The Neal standard asks not whether the insurer acted with malice in the colloquial sense, but whether the insurer knew or should have known that its conduct would cause harm and proceeded anyway. A twenty-one-day denial of a catastrophic-injury pedestrian claim, issued without obtaining the foundational records of a police-attended collision, while the insured's own 911 confession sat unrequested in a government records database, satisfies the Neal standard. The conduct was not careless. It was institutional. It was systematic. It reflected a business decision to close the file quickly rather than investigate honestly.

In Brandt v. Superior Court (1985) 37 Cal.3d 813, the Supreme Court held that attorney fees incurred by a policyholder to obtain the benefits due under an insurance policy are recoverable as an element of tort damages in a bad faith action. The Brandt principle connects directly to the Prentice tort-of-another doctrine: when an insurer's wrongful denial forces the claimant into litigation, the costs of that litigation are compensable damages. Every dollar Rosario spent on the forced litigation, every deposition fee, every filing cost, every hour of his damaged brain devoted to proving what the driver had already confessed; traces back to the February 25, 2021 denial letter.

The pattern CSAA followed has a name in the insurance industry literature. Scholars, regulators, and investigative journalists have identified it as the "deny, delay, defend" model. The model was most famously documented in the internal consulting materials produced by McKinsey & Company for Allstate Insurance in the 1990s. Those materials, which became public through litigation, revealed a systematic strategy: deny claims early, before the claimant has evidence or counsel; delay the litigation to exhaust the claimant's resources; defend aggressively to extract a result below the claim's true value. The McKinsey strategy treated claims handling as a profit center. Every claim denied was revenue preserved. Every claimant who gave up was money saved. The model was not secret. It was taught. It was implemented. It was profitable.

The United States Supreme Court confronted the consequences of this model in State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins. Co. v. Campbell (2003) 538 U.S. 408. The Campbell case exposed a nationwide scheme in which State Farm had systematically denied legitimate claims, fabricated evidence, and destroyed documents to avoid paying what it owed. The Utah jury awarded $145 million in punitive damages. The Supreme Court reduced the award on due process grounds but affirmed that State Farm's conduct was reprehensible; finding that the insurer had engaged in "a nationwide practice" of "weights and tipping" claims to artificially reduce payouts. The parallels to CSAA's conduct are direct: a categorical denial issued before the evidence was gathered, followed by years of suppression to prevent the truth from reaching the judicial system.

The UNUM/Provident disability insurance scandal of the early 2000s revealed another variation of the same pattern. Federal and state regulators found that UNUM had systematically denied legitimate long-term disability claims using internal protocols designed to minimize payouts regardless of the medical evidence. The company's claims handlers were evaluated and compensated based on denial rates. The institutional incentive structure made honest claims evaluation impossible. A multi-state regulatory settlement followed. The parallels to CSAA's conduct are structural: when an insurer's institutional processes are designed to produce denials rather than accurate assessments, the individual denial letters those processes generate are instruments of institutional fraud.

CSAA's February 25, 2021 denial letter fits squarely within this documented pattern. The twenty-one-day timeline. The absence of any records request. The template language of investigative completeness. The categorical denial of a claim that a genuine investigation would have revealed was supported by the insured's own words. Every element of the denial letter maps onto the deny-delay-defend model with the precision of a case study. The question for the court is not whether California law prohibits this conduct. California law has prohibited it since Gruenberg in 1973, reinforced it through Egan in 1979, and authorized punitive consequences through Neal in 1978. The question is whether CSAA believed, in 2021, that it could do what Gruenberg and Egan and Neal said it could not; and whether the five-year litigation that followed proves it was right to think so.

### The Pressure Instrument

The denial letter's function was not merely to communicate a legal position. It was designed; whether by deliberate drafting or by institutional practice: to function as a final word. A claimant who receives a letter from a multi-billion-dollar insurer stating that the investigation is "concluded" and the insured is "not liable" is being told, in institutional terms: this is over. You lost. Go away. The letter carried no invitation to respond. It disclosed no gap in the investigation. It gave no indication that the "conclusion" rested on nothing but the insured's self-serving account. Its purpose was to induce the injured, unrepresented claimant to abandon the claim before retaining counsel and before understanding that the 911 recording, the body-worn camera footage, and the eyewitness statement had never been reviewed. Rosario later confirmed in his declaration that he relied on the letter's representation of investigative finality: he did not seek legal representation immediately, did not seek police or 911 records, and delayed taking action because a major insurance company had told him it had already carefully reviewed the facts and the answer was no.

### The Five-Instrument Proof

The proof that the denial was false when made does not depend on any single source. It is established by five independent facts from the case record. First, the Dolan Law Firm obtained the 911 recording through a routine SFPD public records request on May 12, 2021. Less than three months after CSAA declared the investigation "concluded." Second, CSAA's own retained defense counsel Alberto Reyna subpoenaed the SFPD in February 2024 for the same 911 audio, body-worn camera footage, and CAD data. Proving that three years after the denial, CSAA's agents were still seeking the records CSAA had claimed to have already reviewed. Third, Abdelhalim authenticated the 911 recording at trial on April 17, 2025, answering "Yes. That is my voice". Confirming the recording existed, was obtainable, and contained his voice. Fourth, Abdelhalim acknowledged the collision at his September 30, 2022 deposition, answering "Yes" when asked "Do you recall that collision?" and describing the incident in detail, demonstrating that a genuine investigation would have found a contested liability question that could not be "concluded" in twenty-one days without the foundational records. Fifth, the binary trap closes every exit: either CSAA had the 911 recording and heard its insured say "I barely hit him" and still issued a denial; knowing fraud: or CSAA did not have the recording and the "concluded" representation was false when made. Either path proves the claim. The defense cannot occupy both positions simultaneously, and the record does not offer a third.

## PART V: FORCED INTO LITIGATION: THE RELIANCE AND ITS CONSEQUENCES

### What the Denial Letter Took Away

On February 4, 2021, Subhi Abdelhalim struck Rosario from behind in a crosswalk. On a 911 call placed that same afternoon, Abdelhalim told the dispatcher: "I barely hit him." An independent eyewitness, Justin Rosemond, told Officer Thompson on body-worn camera: "the guy in the van hit the guy laying down on the ground." Under ordinary circumstances. Where an insurer performs its legal duty to investigate a police-attended collision, a party admission on a 911 call combined with an independent eyewitness account should have produced a liability determination in the claimant's favor and payment toward medical bills, lost wages, and related damages. That is the entire purpose of third-party commercial vehicle liability insurance. The $500,000 policy existed for exactly this situation: a driver strikes a pedestrian, the driver is at fault, and the insurer pays the injured party. Instead, twenty-one days later, CSAA Insurance Exchange sent Rosario a letter telling him the investigation was "concluded" and the insured was "not liable." The letter cut off the administrative path to recovery. There was no appeal mechanism. There was no invitation to submit additional evidence. There was no acknowledgment that foundational records (the 911 recording, the body-worn camera footage, the dispatch logs) had never been obtained. The letter communicated finality. The insurer had spoken. The answer was no.

### The But-For Test: What Would Have Happened Without the Denial

But for the denial letter, the trajectory of this case would have been fundamentally different. Abdelhalim admitted fault on the 911 call. Rosemond confirmed the collision on body-worn camera. The police responded, generated a dispatch record, and created five body-worn camera recordings. The evidence of liability was not circumstantial or ambiguous. It was a party admission, corroborated by an independent eyewitness, preserved in the public record of a law enforcement response.

Had CSAA performed the investigation it claimed to have "concluded" (had it obtained the 911 recording, listened to its own insured say "I barely hit him," reviewed the eyewitness account, and examined the dispatch log) it could not credibly have denied liability. Under Crisci v. Security Ins. Co. (1967) 66 Cal.2d 425, 429, the California Supreme Court held that an insurer's duty to accept a reasonable settlement is particularly acute when liability is clear and the potential damages may exceed the policy limit. In Crisci, the insurer refused a settlement within policy limits, the case went to trial, and the resulting judgment exceeded the policy. The Supreme Court held the insurer liable for the entire excess judgment. The Crisci principle is direct: when an insurer possesses evidence establishing its insured's liability (as CSAA would have possessed had it requested the 911 recording) the duty to settle is not discretionary. It is mandatory. The claim would have been processed. A settlement would have been reached. Rosario's medical bills would have been paid through the $500,000 policy. He would have had the resources to seek the medical treatment he needed: follow-up surgeries, rehabilitation, ongoing care for the traumatic brain injury and spinal damage. He would have returned to work. He would have honored his contracts. He would have resumed his career as a senior AI systems architect earning between $550,000 and $1,000,000 per year. None of that happened. Instead, CSAA's false denial set in motion a five-year chain of compounding losses that consumed Rosario's health, his career, his finances, and his capacity to recover from the injuries the driver had caused.

### The Three Forms of Reliance

Rosario relied on the denial letter in three distinct and independently actionable ways. Each is a cognizable form of reliance under California fraud law. Under Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638, a fraud claim requires actual, justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation. Reliance can take the form of inaction (the plaintiff refrains from acting because of the misrepresentation), affirmative action (the plaintiff takes a specific course of action in response to the misrepresentation), or forced action (the plaintiff is compelled into a course of conduct that would not have been necessary absent the misrepresentation). All three forms apply here.

#### Reliance by Inaction

In his declaration, Rosario stated under penalty of perjury: "Because I believed CSAA's February 25, 2021 statement that it had concluded a careful investigation, I did not seek legal representation, and I did not seek Police or 911 records, and I delayed return to work in order to prepare for addressing the CSAA fraud." He delayed retaining counsel. He delayed making public records requests. He delayed consummating job offers and work projects. He understood the letter to mean what it said: that CSAA had already gathered the core incident information, reviewed it, and made a final determination. A reasonable claimant receiving that letter from a major insurer would reach the same conclusion. The reliance was passive and justifiable: the most powerful institutional actor in the room said it had done its homework, and the claimant believed it.

This form of reliance is well established in California law. Under Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 976-977, a defendant's misrepresentation that induces the plaintiff to delay taking action constitutes actionable reliance when the delay proximately causes harm. CSAA's letter induced Rosario to delay seeking the very evidence, the 911 recording; that would have established liability and forced a settlement. The delay was the direct product of the misrepresentation.

#### Reliance by Forced Litigation

When Rosario eventually understood that CSAA's denial was categorical and final, that the insurer would not pay, would not negotiate, and would not acknowledge the collision; the only path to covering his medical bills, recovering lost wages, and obtaining compensation for a traumatic brain injury, five fractured ribs, a collapsed lung, spinal damage, and disfigurement ran through the San Francisco Superior Court. There was no alternative dispute resolution. There was no administrative remedy. There was no settlement process. CSAA had closed every door except the courthouse. Rosario retained the Dolan Law Firm. On July 27, 2021, the firm filed CGC-21-594102. The litigation that followed would last approximately five years.

This is the reliance the Prentice doctrine recognizes. In Prentice v. North American Title Guar. Corp. (1963) 59 Cal.2d 618, the California Supreme Court held that when a defendant's tortious conduct forces the plaintiff into litigation with a third party, the costs of that forced litigation are recoverable as consequential damages. The Prentice "tort-of-another" rule treats the entire litigation as a foreseeable consequence of the fraud. Every deposition, every motion, every hearing, every day of trial, and every dollar of attorney fees traces back to the false denial that made the litigation necessary. But for the denial letter, the litigation would not have occurred. The claim would have been resolved through the insurance process the policy was designed to provide.

#### Reliance on the Continued Suppression During Litigation

The third form of reliance is the most insidious. Once Rosario was inside the litigation, he relied on the institutional representations of the defense and the court that the process was fair and the evidence was being properly exchanged. Instead, CSAA's retained defense counsel received the 911 audio, the body-worn camera footage, and the CAD logs at Rosario's deposition on August 18, 2022; and then spent the next three years claiming not to know where those materials came from. The defense filed Motion in Limine No. 17 claiming the 911 recording was of unknown provenance. Defense counsel told the court: "this is news to me." Defense counsel told the court: "I am not sure what recording he is referring to." Each representation extended the litigation, blocked the evidence from reaching the jury in proper context, and compounded the damage.

But for the continued suppression and misrepresentation of the evidence during the litigation, a settlement could have been reached at any point. The evidence of liability was in the defense file from 2022 forward. A party admission on a 911 call, an independent eyewitness on body-worn camera, and the defendant's own deposition acknowledgment of the collision constituted an overwhelming liability case. Any good-faith assessment of that evidence by any competent defense attorney would have produced a recommendation to settle within policy limits. The defense did not make that recommendation. The insurer did not settle. The suppression continued. And Rosario was left to litigate for years to prove what every party to the litigation already knew.

### The Criminal Dimension: Abdelhalim's Unreported Felony

The driver's conduct on February 4, 2021 was not merely negligent. It was criminal. Under California Vehicle Code section 20001(a), the driver of any vehicle involved in an accident resulting in injury to or death of any person shall immediately stop the vehicle at the scene and shall fulfill certain requirements, including providing identification and rendering reasonable assistance to any injured person. Abdelhalim did not stop. He struck Rosario in the crosswalk, looked at him through the passenger window as Rosario lay on the ground, and drove the van down the street to conceal it. He fled the scene at 2:41 PM without calling 911 or providing assistance. He did not return until 2:49 PM, eight minutes later: and when he did, he offered Rosario a five-dollar bill.

Under California Vehicle Code section 20001(b)(2), a hit-and-run resulting in permanent, serious injury is a felony punishable by imprisonment in the state prison for two, three, or four years, or in a county jail for not less than 90 days nor more than one year, or by a fine of not less than one thousand dollars nor more than ten thousand dollars, or by both imprisonment and fine. Rosario's injuries; traumatic brain injury, five fractured ribs, collapsed lung, spinal damage. Constitute permanent serious injury within the meaning of the statute.

When first responders arrived, Abdelhalim lied. He told Officer DeLeon he had no contact with and no involvement in the incident. He denied hitting the victim to the EMTs. He contradicted his own 911 admission. Under Penal Code section 148.5(a), knowingly filing a false report with a peace officer is a misdemeanor. Under Penal Code section 118, perjury and false statements to law enforcement are criminal offenses. Abdelhalim's failure to truthfully report the collision to the responding officers was not a minor procedural lapse. It was obstruction. It was a form of concealed hit-and-run: returning to the scene but refusing to identify himself as the driver to law enforcement.

There is no statute of limitations for a felony hit-and-run resulting in permanent serious injury under California Vehicle Code section 20001(b)(2). The criminal exposure remains open. The SFPD released Abdelhalim at 3:15 PM on the day of the collision despite eyewitness testimony, the driver's 911 confession, and evidence of a felony. No arrest was made. No citation was issued. No vehicle was impounded. The failure to prosecute does not extinguish the criminal nature of the conduct. It reinforces the institutional failure at every level: the police let the driver go, the insurer denied the claim without investigating, and the defense suppressed the evidence for years.

### What the Litigation Was For

The litigation was not abstract. Rosario was not litigating a commercial dispute or a contract interpretation. He was litigating to prove what the driver had already confessed; that a collision occurred. Because the insurance company that was supposed to pay his medical bills said no before even listening to the 911 call where its own insured admitted fault. He was litigating against a driver who had committed a felony hit-and-run, lied to police, and was never arrested. And he was doing it because the insurance company that held a $500,000 policy for exactly this type of incident refused to honor that policy.

On February 5, 2021, the day after the collision, Rosario had severe chest pain. X-rays at One Medical showed five broken ribs and a pneumothorax, a collapsed lung. He was transferred by ambulance to Zuckerberg San Francisco General Hospital, where he underwent surgery. He remained approximately four days. He was discharged with instructions for bedrest: no lifting, reaching, or pulling. He was bedridden for approximately two months. The traumatic brain injury resulted in increased cognitive overhead and reduced sustained focus capacity.

None of these medical costs were covered. CSAA's denial letter, issued while Rosario was still in recovery, told him the insured was not liable. The surgery, the hospital stay, the ambulance transfer, the follow-up care at One Medical, Kaiser, and Blue Buddha, all of it became Rosario's burden alone. Without money to pay for additional surgeries, ongoing treatment, or rehabilitation, Rosario could not put his life back together. The injuries did not stop compounding because the insurer refused to pay. They compounded faster. The denial letter did not merely deny a claim. It denied Rosario the capacity to heal.

### The Dolan Law Firm: Failure of Duty or Alignment of Interest

The Dolan Law Firm retained the case, obtained the 911 recording on May 12, 2021, obtained the body-worn camera footage and CAD logs, and produced all of it to the defense at Rosario's deposition on August 18, 2022. The firm had material positive evidence that the driver was liable: the party admission confession on the 911 call and the eyewitness statement on the police body cam. This evidence was never disclosed to Rosario. The Dolan Law Firm never told its own client about the 911 admission. It never confronted CSAA or its retained defense counsel with the party admission. It never used the strongest evidence in the case as leverage in settlement negotiations. It focused on lost wages and medical records while the dispositive liability evidence sat in the file, unused.

In 2023, when the Dolan Law Firm demanded CSAA pay the policy limit of $500,000 and CSAA refused, the Dolan Law Firm did not escalate. It did not file a bad faith action. It did not threaten to expose the 911 admission in open court. It turned on its own client. The firm demanded that Rosario accept a settlement of $250,000, less than half of the $500,000 policy limit. It threatened to drop Rosario if he refused. Rosario refused. The firm withdrew in August 2024, leaving Rosario to represent himself at trial against an institutional defense funded by CSAA.

The Dolan Law Firm either failed in its legal duty to its client or acted in alignment with CSAA's interest in making the case go away cheaply. Under California Rules of Professional Conduct, Rule 1.4, an attorney has a duty to promptly inform the client of significant developments in the matter, including the discovery of material evidence favorable to the client's position. The 911 recording containing the insured's party admission was the single most important piece of evidence in the case. The failure to disclose it to Rosario was either professional negligence of the most fundamental kind or a deliberate choice to suppress evidence that would have given Rosario leverage to demand. And receive, the full policy limit.

Whether the explanation is incompetence or collusion, the practical effect was identical: Rosario was kept in the dark about the strongest evidence in his own case while his law firm pressured him to settle for a fraction of what the evidence supported. When he refused, they abandoned him. CSAA benefited from both outcomes. If Rosario had accepted $250,000, CSAA would have resolved a $500,000 policy limit case for half the exposure. When Rosario was abandoned, CSAA faced a brain-injured pro per plaintiff at trial instead of an informed attorney armed with a party admission, which was an even better outcome for the insurer. The Dolan Law Firm's withdrawal, whether by negligence or design, served CSAA's interest at the expense of the injured client it was duty-bound to protect.

### The But-For Cascade: What Rosario Could Have Avoided

But for CSAA's misrepresentation in the denial letter and the failure to settle in good faith when the policy limit was demanded, Rosario could have avoided:

**Years of litigation.** The case would have settled. The evidence was overwhelming. A genuine pre-denial investigation would have identified liability. A good-faith response to the policy limit demand in 2023 (when the defense had the 911 recording, the body-worn camera footage, and the defendant's deposition acknowledgment) would have ended the case. Instead, the litigation lasted five years.

**Loss of business opportunities.** Rosario lost the Raffin Group engagement (estimated at $3 to $5 million), the Google GPrime investment (approximately $500,000 in net loss), and forty-eight months of income at an average pre-injury rate of approximately $30,000 per month, totaling approximately $1.4 million in lost income alone; excluding the canceled contracts.

**Loss of wages.** During the forty-eight-month delay period, Rosario's income dropped from approximately $30,000 per month to approximately zero. The time he would have spent earning income was consumed by the litigation: identifying record sources, making public records requests, preparing subpoenas, managing authentication disputes, attending hearings, and preparing for trial.

**Costs of litigation, depositions, and attorneys.** The Dolan Law Firm's fees, the costs of multiple depositions (Rosario, Abdelhalim, Officer Fernandez, Raffin, Victoria Rosario, and others), the costs of subpoenas, document production, and trial preparation; all of it was a foreseeable, proximately caused consequence of the fraudulent denial.

**Extenuation of pain and suffering.** Without money from the insurance policy, Rosario could not afford medical treatments he needed. He could not afford additional surgeries. He could not afford sustained rehabilitation for the traumatic brain injury or the spinal damage. The denial letter did not cause the injuries. Abdelhalim caused the injuries. But the denial letter caused the inability to treat those injuries. It left Rosario with no money, no medical coverage from the liable party's insurer, and no capacity to put his life back online. The pain and suffering that should have been mitigated by timely medical care, funded by the policy that existed for that purpose; was instead prolonged and compounded by the insurer's refusal to pay.

**Abandonment by counsel.** But for the denial and the failure to settle, Rosario would not have been in the position where the Dolan Law Firm could pressure him to accept a below-value settlement and then abandon him when he refused. A good-faith settlement at the policy limit in 2023 would have ended the case. There would have been no need for the Dolan Law Firm to withdraw. There would have been no pro per plaintiff at trial. There would have been no 9-3 verdict. The Dolan withdrawal was itself a consequence of the protracted litigation caused by the denial.

### The Prentice Doctrine: Litigation Costs as Consequential Damages

This causal chain is not merely a narrative argument. It is a cognizable legal theory under California law. In Prentice v. North American Title Guar. Corp. (1963) 59 Cal.2d 618, the California Supreme Court held that a plaintiff may recover attorney fees and litigation costs incurred in a third-party action when those costs were proximately caused by the defendant's tortious conduct. The Prentice doctrine is sometimes called the "tort-of-another" rule: when a defendant's fraud forces the plaintiff into litigation with a third party, the costs of that forced litigation are recoverable as consequential damages.

Rosario's First Amended Complaint in CGC-25-631802 pleads Prentice damages. The Memorandum in Support states: "CSAA's fraudulent denial letter forced Plaintiff into litigation to obtain and authenticate evidence the insurer claimed it had already investigated. The entire cost of that forced litigation is a foreseeable, proximately caused consequence of the denial." The complaint segregates damages to defeat duplication arguments: Prentice attorney-fee damages are separated from lost earnings, fraud-delta damages (incremental harm distinct from the underlying collision), and punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294.

### The Devastating Simplification

The entire forced-litigation reliance theory reduces to a single statement: CSAA's driver admitted on 911 that he hit Rosario. CSAA never listened to that recording. CSAA told Rosario the driver was not liable. And Rosario was forced to litigate for five years, through a collapsed lung, brain damage, and lost income: simply to prove what the driver had already confessed, because the insurance company that was supposed to pay his medical bills said no. His own lawyers hid the strongest evidence from him, pressured him to settle for half the policy, and abandoned him when he refused. He went to trial alone, brain-injured, against an institutional defense that had the evidence for years and told the court it was "news to me." The jury voted 9-3: one juror away from a different result. Every dollar, every year, every lost contract, every untreated injury, and every hour of litigation traces back to the February 25, 2021 denial letter and the institutional decision to deny, suppress, and defend rather than investigate, acknowledge, and pay.

### 2023: Demand for Policy Limit and AAA's Refusal

At some point in 2023, the Dolan Law Firm, on behalf of Rosario, demanded that the insurance company pay the policy limit of $500,000. CSAA rejected the demand. Under California law, an insurer that refuses a reasonable settlement offer within policy limits when liability is clear may be estopped from later contesting coverage or may face extracontractual liability. In Merritt v. Reserve Ins. Co. (1973) 34 Cal.App.3d 858, the court held that an insured whose insurer unreasonably refuses to settle may assign the bad faith claim to the injured claimant. Meaning that the very person the insurer harmed by refusing to settle can pursue the bad faith claim directly against the insurer. The Merritt assignment doctrine transforms the insurer's refusal to settle from a dispute between the insurer and its insured into a direct liability to the injured claimant. Under Johansen v. California State Auto. Assn. Inter-Ins. Bureau (1975) 15 Cal.3d 9, the Supreme Court confirmed that an insurer's obligation to settle is measured by whether a prudent insurer without policy limits would have accepted the settlement. The test is objective: not what CSAA believed about its exposure, but what a reasonable insurer with the same evidence would have done. A reasonable insurer holding the 911 recording, the eyewitness statement, and the insured's deposition acknowledgment of the collision would have settled within the $500,000 policy limit. CSAA's refusal was not a legitimate coverage dispute. It was a continuation of the institutional denial strategy that began with the February 25, 2021 letter. ### The Implied Covenant Breach: Hamilton, Comunale, and the Duty That Cannot Be Waived

The refusal to settle within policy limits when liability is clear has been condemned by California courts for more than six decades. The implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is not a suggestion. It is a legal obligation read into every insurance contract by operation of law, meaning the parties do not need to negotiate for it, cannot waive it, and cannot contract around it. In Hamilton v. Maryland Casualty Co. (2002) 27 Cal.4th 718, the California Supreme Court held that an insurer that refuses a reasonable settlement offer within policy limits when liability is reasonably clear exposes itself to liability for the full amount of any resulting judgment, even if that judgment exceeds the policy limit. The Hamilton principle creates a powerful incentive for honest claims evaluation: an insurer that gambles with its insured's exposure by refusing to settle a clear-liability case does so at its own financial risk. The doctrine has its roots in Comunale v. Traders & General Ins. Co. (1958) 50 Cal.2d 654, where the Supreme Court first recognized that the insurer's power to control the defense creates a corresponding duty to protect the insured's interests. An insurer that prioritizes its own financial interest, avoiding payment. Over its insured's interest in resolving a clear-liability case within policy limits has breached the most fundamental obligation the law imposes on it. The principle announced in Comunale in 1958 has been reaffirmed, expanded, and applied in hundreds of California cases over the subsequent seven decades. It is not new law. It is not evolving law. It is settled, mandatory, and inescapable.

By the time CSAA refused the $500,000 policy limit demand, the evidentiary record was unambiguous. Its retained counsel had received, at the August 18, 2022 deposition of Rosario, the full Dolan production: 911 audio, five body-worn camera videos, and the CAD log. That production included Abdelhalim saying "I barely hit him" on the 911 call, a party admission under Evidence Code section 1240. It included Officer Thompson's body-worn camera recording of Justin Rosemond stating "the guy in the van hit the guy laying down on the ground." And it included the defendant's own deposition testimony of September 30, 2022, in which Abdelhalim acknowledged the collision when asked "Do you recall that collision?" and answered "Yes." Every insurance policy in California contains an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, read into the contract by operation of law under Comunale v. Traders & General Ins. Co. (1958) 50 Cal.2d 654. That covenant required CSAA to settle a clear-liability case within policy limits. When liability is established by the insured's own admission on a 911 call, an independent eyewitness on police body cam, and the insured's own deposition acknowledgment, refusing to pay the policy limit is not a legitimate coverage position. It is a breach. Under Hamilton v. Maryland Casualty Co. (2002) 27 Cal.4th 718 and Merritt v. Reserve Ins. Co. (1973) 34 Cal.App.3d 858, an insurer that refuses a reasonable settlement demand within policy limits when liability is clear may be exposed to judgment in excess of the policy, and the insured can assign that bad faith claim to the injured claimant. The refusal to pay the policy limit in 2023 was not a business decision made in good faith. It was the continuation of a strategy that began with the February 25, 2021 denial letter: deny the evidence, deny liability, and force the claimant to litigate what the insured had already confessed.

Rosario had submitted proposed jury instructions including CACI 700 (Vehicular Basic Standard of Care), CACI 707 (Driver's Duty to Pedestrians in Roadway), and CACI 721 (Driver's Duty After Accident), all of which were tied to Vehicle Code sections 22107 and 21804(a) governing turning movements and right-of-way duties. The court gave CACI 400 and 401 (general negligence instructions) but refused to instruct the jury on CACI 707 and 721. The specific vehicular duties governing a driver's obligation to yield before turning, directly applicable to Abdelhalim's right turn onto Tehama, were withheld from the jury.

The same day, the court allowed into the jury room the plaintiff's original Complaint as a defense exhibit. The Complaint contained allegations expressly referencing Vehicle Code sections 22107 and 21804(a) as the statutory basis for defendant's negligence per se. The court thus simultaneously admitted a document citing the Vehicle Code as substantive evidence and declined to instruct the jury on the same Vehicle Code provisions.

During closing argument, the defense argued from the plaintiff's own Complaint, now in the jury room as an exhibit. Plaintiff argued that turning vehicles had a duty to yield. The jury received the exhibit containing the Vehicle Code references but received no instruction on how to apply those statutes. The resulting instructions (CACI 400 and 401 without CACI 707 or 721) left the jury with a general negligence framework stripped of the specific vehicular duties that governed this exact type of collision: a turning vehicle at an intersection with a yield sign, striking a pedestrian in a crosswalk.

### April 23, 2025 (Trial Day 11): The Jury Question and the Verdict

During deliberations, the jury asked: "Should we consider the Vehicle Code?" The court responded that the Vehicle Code provisions were "not part of the evidence" and "not permitted to consider." The jury had wanted to apply the law. They were told they could not. This is the instructional error that anchors Appeal No. A173827. The jury returned a verdict of nine to three for the defense. Nine to three. The minimum civil majority under California law. A single juror changing sides would have altered the outcome. Under Soule v. General Motors Corp. (1994) 8 Cal.4th 548, the California Supreme Court held that a trial court has a sua sponte duty to instruct on any point of law relevant to the issues raised by the evidence. The failure to do so is reversible error when it is reasonably probable the error affected the outcome. In Newing v. Cheatham (1975) 15 Cal.3d 351, the Supreme Court held that error in refusing to give a requested instruction on a material issue is prejudicial when the jury might have reached a different verdict. In Osborn v. Irwin Memorial Blood Bank (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 234, the Court of Appeal held that negligence per se instructions are mandatory when there is evidence that the defendant violated a statute designed to prevent the type of injury that occurred, which is precisely the situation here, where Vehicle Code sections 22107 and 21804(a) govern turning movements at intersections and were designed to protect pedestrians in crosswalks. For a nine-to-three verdict, the absolute minimum civil majority, the probability of a different result absent the instructional error is substantial. The verdict was signed and dated April 23, 2025. The jury poll showed nine jurors confirming the verdict; three did not raise their hands.

## PART XIII: BAD FAITH: THE CASE AGAINST CSAA UNDER CALIFORNIA LAW

### The Governing Legal Standard

California imposes a duty of good faith and fair dealing on every insurer. This duty is not voluntary. It is implied by law into every insurance policy as a matter of statutory and common-law obligation. The duty runs from the insurer to both its insured and, in the third-party liability context, to the injured claimant whose rights are derivative of the policy.

The two primary sources of the bad faith duty are statutory and common law. The statutory framework is codified in California Insurance Code section 790.03(h), which defines specific unfair claims settlement practices. A single knowing violation, or a pattern of violations, is actionable. The common-law framework derives from Comunale v. Traders & General Ins. Co. (1958) 50 Cal.2d 654, which holds that every insurance contract carries an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Breach of this covenant gives rise to tort liability; not merely contract damages. Under Gruenberg v. Aetna Ins. Co. (1973) 9 Cal.3d 566, the duty not to unreasonably withhold policy benefits is a duty imposed by law, and its breach constitutes a tort.

### Failure to Investigate: Insurance Code Section 790.03(h)(3)

California Insurance Code section 790.03(h)(3) makes it an unlawful unfair insurance practice to "fail to adopt and implement reasonable standards for the prompt investigation and processing of claims arising under insurance policies."

The February 25, 2021 denial letter stated the investigation was "concluded." The claim file contained no record of a request for the 911 recording, a request for body-worn camera footage, a request for CAD logs, a request for the police supplemental report, or contact with the SFPD or the Department of Emergency Management. These are the foundational records of any police-attended vehicle-pedestrian collision. They were public records, obtainable through a routine written request. The Dolan Law Firm paralegal obtained the 911 recording on May 12, 2021; less than three months after the denial. CSAA, a multi-billion-dollar institutional insurer with dedicated claims departments, declared the investigation "concluded" in twenty-one days without making a single records request.

Under Egan v. Mutual of Omaha Ins. Co. (1979) 24 Cal.3d 809, 819, an insurer cannot reasonably deny a claim when it has failed to investigate the facts upon which its denial is based. An insurer that denies without investigating has, by definition, failed to adopt reasonable standards for the prompt investigation of claims. CSAA's conduct satisfies this element of bad faith.

### False Representation of Investigative Completeness

The denial letter used the word "concluded" twice and the phrase "carefully reviewing" once. These are not casual expressions of opinion. They are affirmative representations about a past, completed process: that CSAA obtained relevant evidence, reviewed it, and reached a determination based on that review. Under Neu-Visions Sports, Inc. v. Soren/McAdam/Bartells (2000) 86 Cal.App.4th 303, 309-310, a statement is actionable when it "expressly or impliedly asserts a specific verifiable fact." The assertion that an investigation was "concluded" is a verifiable fact: either CSAA obtained and reviewed the foundational evidence, or it did not. The claim file answers the question. The assertion was false when made.

This false representation is independently actionable as common-law fraud and as a breach of the implied covenant. Under the implied covenant, an insurer must give at least as much consideration to the claimant's interests as to its own. Issuing a categorical denial before obtaining the foundational evidence of the incident inverts that duty entirely. CSAA placed its financial interest; closing the file: above its obligation to conduct a genuine inquiry.

### Failure to Explain the Basis for Denial: Insurance Code Section 790.03(h)(13)

California Insurance Code section 790.03(h)(13) requires an insurer to promptly provide a reasonable explanation of the basis in the insurance policy for a denial of a claim. The February 25 letter stated the investigation was "concluded" and the insured was "not liable" without identifying a single piece of evidence reviewed, a single witness contacted, or a single record obtained. A claimant receiving that letter had no way to evaluate whether the determination was correct or how to contest it. The letter communicated finality without substance. This violates (h)(13)'s requirement.

### Failure to Settle When Liability Was Reasonably Clear: Insurance Code Section 790.03(h)(5)

California Insurance Code section 790.03(h)(5) prohibits an insurer from "not attempting in good faith to effectuate prompt, fair, and equitable settlements of claims in which liability has become reasonably clear."

By 2023 at the latest, liability was reasonably clear by any objective measure. The insured's own party admission on the 911 call stated: "I barely hit him." An independent eyewitness statement on body-worn camera stated: "the guy in the van hit the guy laying down on the ground." The insured's own deposition acknowledgment on September 30, 2022 confirmed the collision when asked "Do you recall that collision?" and the insured answered "Yes." The Dolan Law Firm demanded the $500,000 policy limit. CSAA refused.

Under Crisci v. Security Ins. Co. (1967) 66 Cal.2d 425, 429, an insurer's duty to accept a reasonable settlement offer becomes particularly acute when liability is clear and the damages may exceed the policy limit. The insurer cannot gamble with the insured's exposure. CSAA did exactly that: it refused to settle a case where the insured had admitted fault, an eyewitness confirmed the collision, and the defendant's own deposition contradicted his later trial testimony. The refusal was not a legitimate coverage dispute. It was a continuation of the denial strategy.

Under Wilson v. 21st Century Ins. Co. (2007) 42 Cal.4th 713, bad faith is established where the insurer fails to investigate and denies on an inadequate factual record. The failure to investigate in February 2021 and the failure to settle in 2023 are the same bad faith: one continuous institutional decision to deny rather than pay.

### Unreasonable Denial Under All Circumstances: The Waller Standard

Under Waller v. Truck Ins. Exchange, Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 1, 36, the test for bad faith is whether the insurer's conduct was unreasonable under the circumstances. The relevant question is not whether CSAA believed its denial was correct. The question is whether a reasonable insurer, investigating in good faith, could have reached the same conclusion.

The answer is no. A reasonable insurer investigating a vehicle-pedestrian collision where police responded, a 911 call was generated, and body-worn camera footage was recorded would have obtained those records before making any liability determination. A reasonable insurer that heard its own insured say "I barely hit him" on the 911 recording could not have concluded the insured was "not liable." A reasonable insurer that possessed the Dolan production (from August 2022 forward) and a deposition where the insured acknowledged the collision (September 2022) would have recommended settlement within policy limits rather than continued denial.

CSAA did not act as a reasonable insurer at any point in the five-year chain. The denial was unreasonable when made. The refusal to settle was unreasonable when the evidence was in the defense file. The continued suppression of the evidence at trial was unreasonable under any standard.

### Suppression of Evidence During Litigation: The Neal Standard and Punitive Damages

The bad faith did not end with the denial letter. It continued into the litigation. CSAA's retained defense counsel received the 911 audio, the body-worn camera footage, and the CAD logs at Rosario's deposition on August 18, 2022. From that date forward, the defense file contained a party admission, an independent eyewitness account, and the defendant's own deposition acknowledgment of the collision. Despite possessing this evidence, CSAA's defense counsel spent three years claiming ignorance. Motion in Limine No. 17 (January 17, 2025) claimed the 911 recording was produced "at the eleventh hour" and that the defendant had "no idea" where it came from: signed under penalty of perjury. At the January 27 MSC, Navaratnasingham told the court: "our office did not receive the 911 call until the day of the MSC"; "this is news to me." At trial on April 16, 2025, Reyna told the court: "I am not sure what recording he is referring to". Fourteen months after subpoenaing that exact recording by name from the SFPD.

Each representation extended the litigation, blocked the evidence from reaching the jury in proper context, and compounded the damage. This is not a discovery dispute. It is an extension of the original bad faith denial into the courtroom: denying the evidence exists to prevent the claim from being resolved on its merits.

Under Neal v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1978) 21 Cal.3d 910, punitive damages are available when the insurer's conduct reflects a conscious disregard of the claimant's rights. The progression from denial without investigation in 2021 to suppression of evidence at trial in 2025 establishes that disregard. The conduct was not careless. It was systematic. It was institutional. And it was sustained over five years.

### The Criminal Dimension Amplifies the Bad Faith

The driver committed a felony hit-and-run under California Vehicle Code section 20001(b)(2). He fled the scene. He lied to responding officers. He contradicted his own 911 admission. He was released without arrest or citation. CSAA insured this driver under a commercial vehicle liability policy. When a policyholder commits a felony in the operation of a covered vehicle, the insurer's duty to investigate is heightened, not diminished. The existence of a criminal hit-and-run. Which CSAA could have discovered by obtaining the 911 recording or the body-worn camera footage. Made the denial even more unreasonable. An insurer that declines to investigate whether its insured committed a felony and then categorically denies liability is not exercising reasonable business judgment. It is shielding a criminal driver from the consequences of his conduct at the expense of the injured claimant. There is no statute of limitations for a felony hit-and-run resulting in permanent serious injury under Vehicle Code section 20001(b)(2). The criminal exposure remains open.

### The Three Forms of Reliance Connect Bad Faith to Damages

Bad faith is not merely a finding of wrongful conduct. It is a finding that the wrongful conduct caused harm. Each of the three reliance forms described in Part V connects CSAA's bad faith to specific, documented damages.

Inaction reliance establishes that the denial letter delayed Rosario from retaining counsel, seeking the 911 recording, and pursuing the claim promptly. Under Egan, an insurer that delays the claimant's access to benefits through unreasonable denial practices is liable for the resulting harm, including consequential damages from the delay.

Forced litigation reliance establishes that the entire five-year litigation was a direct consequence of the bad faith denial. Under Prentice v. North American Title Guar. Corp. (1963) 59 Cal.2d 618, the litigation costs are themselves recoverable as consequential damages. Under the bad faith tort, the scope of damages is broader than contract: it includes all foreseeable consequential damages, emotional distress, and punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294.

Suppression reliance establishes that the bad faith continued during the litigation. The evidence was in the defense file from 2022 forward. A good-faith assessment of that evidence would have produced a recommendation to settle within policy limits. The defense did not make that recommendation. The insurer did not settle. The suppression continued. Rosario was left to litigate for years to prove what every party to the litigation already knew.

### The Industry Pattern: CSAA's Conduct in Historical Context

CSAA's bad faith did not occur in a vacuum. It occurred within an insurance industry that has been repeatedly caught, condemned, and punished for precisely this pattern of conduct. The historical record is not ambiguous. It is a catalog of institutional abuse that California courts and regulators have been fighting for decades.

The earliest recognition of the bad faith tort in American law came in Hilker v. Western Automobile Ins. Co. (1931) 204 Wis. 1, where the Wisconsin Supreme Court held that an insurer's duty to its insured is quasi-fiduciary, meaning the insurer holds a position of trust and power that the law will not allow it to abuse. The Hilker principle migrated to California through Comunale and became the foundation of the modern bad faith doctrine. For nearly a century, American courts have told insurers: you cannot deny without investigating, you cannot suppress evidence, and you cannot use the power differential between a multi-billion-dollar institution and an injured individual as a weapon. CSAA's conduct in this case violated every principle that Hilker established in 1931.

The Allstate/McKinsey consulting scandal of the 1990s revealed the institutional mechanics behind the deny-delay-defend model. Internal documents produced in litigation showed that McKinsey & Company had advised Allstate to treat claims handling as a profit center. The strategy was explicit: deny claims early, force claimants into litigation, and use the costs and delays of the legal system to extract settlements below the true value of the claims. The McKinsey documents described the strategy using a boxing metaphor: Allstate would put on "boxing gloves" against claimants who refused lowball offers, making the litigation so expensive and time-consuming that most would capitulate. The documents became public through the work of investigative journalists and plaintiffs' attorneys. They revealed what consumer advocates had long suspected: that the insurance industry's claims-handling practices were not the product of individual bad judgment but of deliberate, corporate-level strategy designed to maximize profit at the expense of injured people. CSAA's twenty-one-day denial, followed by five years of litigation during which the insurer's own agents suppressed evidence of the insured's 911 confession, maps onto the McKinsey model with the precision of a case study.

The State Farm catastrophe in Campbell v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins. Co. (2003) 538 U.S. 408 exposed the same institutional pathology at a national scale. The case revealed that State Farm had maintained a national scheme of capping payouts, fabricating evidence, and pressuring employees to deny legitimate claims. The Utah jury awarded $145 million in punitive damages. The Supreme Court reduced the award but affirmed that State Farm's conduct was reprehensible, noting that the insurer had engaged in "trickery and deceit" and had shown "indifference to or a reckless disregard of the health or safety of others." The Campbell facts read like a blueprint for CSAA's conduct: categorical denial without investigation, suppression of evidence favorable to the claimant, and institutional defense strategies designed to prevent the truth from reaching the judicial system.

The UNUM/Provident long-term disability scandal of the early 2000s demonstrated that the pattern was not limited to automobile insurance. Federal and state regulators found that UNUM had systematically denied legitimate disability claims using internal protocols that evaluated claims handlers based on denial rates rather than accuracy. The institutional incentive structure made honest claims evaluation impossible: a claims handler who approved too many claims faced discipline, while one who denied claims efficiently was rewarded. A multi-state regulatory settlement required UNUM to reassess hundreds of thousands of previously denied claims. The structural parallel to CSAA is direct: when an insurer's institutional processes are designed to produce denials rather than honest assessments, the individual denial letters those processes generate are instruments of institutional fraud regardless of the intent of any individual adjuster.

### Conclusion: One Continuous Bad Faith Decision

CSAA's bad faith is not a single act. It is a continuous institutional decision spanning February 25, 2021 through April 25, 2025, from the denial letter to the verdict. The insurer denied without investigating. It refused to settle when the evidence was overwhelming. It suppressed the evidence in court. It forced a brain-injured claimant to litigate for five years to prove what its own insured had already confessed on a 911 call. Under California law (under Insurance Code section 790.03(h), under Comunale, under Egan, under Crisci, under Wilson, under Waller, under Neal, and under Gruenberg) that is not a legitimate coverage dispute. That is bad faith.

The bad faith case against CSAA in CGC-25-631802 is supported by the certified court record, the denial letter itself, the defense subpoena to SFPD, the Jessup testimony, the defendant's trial authentication of the 911 recording, and the binary trap that closes every defense exit. Either CSAA investigated and committed knowing fraud, or CSAA did not investigate and the "concluded" representation was false when made. There is no third option. Either path leads to liability for bad faith, fraud, and consequential damages under the Prentice doctrine, with punitive damages available under Civil Code section 3294 for the conscious disregard of the claimant's rights demonstrated across five years of institutional denial.
